Ethos

Virtue-led Business learning.

Business Studies at Nishkam is not only about passing exams. Every topic connects to who we are — a community learning to serve, to think carefully and to act with integrity.

Pillar I

Faith-Inspired

Learning from the wisdom of all traditions. Businesses that act with integrity and compassion reflect values at the heart of every great faith.

Pillar II

Virtues-Led

Fostering compassion, humility, justice and courage. Every business decision is an ethical choice — a chance to practise virtue or fall short of it.

Pillar III

Aspiring for Excellence

Becoming the best human beings we can possibly be. In Business Studies, this means thinking critically, writing with precision, and achieving Grade 9.

Faith

Business through the lens of faith.

At Nishkam, the world of business is not separate from the world of virtue. Every decision a business makes is an ethical act — and every topic you study connects to the kind of person you are becoming.

The word Nishkam means selfless — acting without desire for personal reward. It comes from the Sikh tradition of Nishkam Seva, selfless service, but its meaning speaks across all faiths. When Guru Nanak Dev Ji was given money by his father to make a good trade, he used it to feed hungry holy men instead. He called it a true and worthwhile investment — sacha sauda. The profit was not financial. The return was human goodness.

This is the lens through which we study business at Nishkam School West London. Yes, we learn about revenue, profit margins and market share. But we also ask: what is a business for? Is it only to make money — or is it to serve people, create livelihoods, treat workers with dignity, and leave the world better than we found it? Every unit in this course gives you the chance to think about both.

Our four form groups — Compassion, Contentment, Humility and Truth — are drawn from the virtues that all great faith traditions share. They are not just words on a wall. They are qualities we practise every day, in every lesson, in the way we speak to each other and in the work we produce. When you write a 12-mark answer about whether a business should close factories to cut costs, you are making an ethical argument — and the virtues you carry with you shape how you think about it.

Compassion

Form Group Virtue

“Those who know their Lord and Master, show compassion; then, they become immortal, and attain the state of eternal dignity.” — Sri Guru Granth Sahib, p. 340

In Business Studies, compassion means considering the impact of decisions on all stakeholders — not just shareholders. When a business chooses to pay a living wage or source materials ethically, it is choosing compassion over convenience. Unit 1 (Stakeholders) and Unit 2 (Ethics) ask you to think this way.

Contentment

Form Group Virtue

“To be content doesn’t mean you don’t desire more, it means you’re thankful for what you have and patient for what’s to come.”

In Business Studies, contentment teaches us that growth for growth’s sake is not always wise. A business that expands too fast without solid foundations may collapse. Unit 1 (Business Planning) and Unit 6 (Finance) teach the discipline of sustainable, patient growth over reckless ambition.

Humility

Form Group Virtue

“True humility is not thinking less of yourself; it is thinking of yourself less.” — C. S. Lewis

In Business Studies, humility means acknowledging what you don’t know and being willing to learn. The best business leaders listen to their employees and customers. In your exam answers, humility means using evidence and data rather than unsupported assertions. Our motto says it all: Man neeva, mat uchi — to be humble, to be wise.

Truth

Form Group Virtue

“Three things cannot be long hidden: the sun, the moon, and the truth.” — Buddha

In Business Studies, truth means being honest in your analysis. Do not write what you think the examiner wants to hear — write what the evidence shows. When a business publishes transparent financial reports or admits a product recall, it is practising truth. Unit 6 (Finance) and Unit 2 (Ethics) reward truthful, evidence-based thinking.

Guru Nanak Dev Ji travelled across the known world for 25 years — not to trade goods, but to trade wisdom. He met people of every faith and background, and in each place he asked the same question: are you living truthfully? That question is at the heart of every business decision you will ever study.
