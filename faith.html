Business through the lens of faith.

At Nishkam, the world of business is not separate from the world of virtue. Every decision a business makes is an ethical act — and every topic you study connects to the kind of person you are becoming.

A True and Worthwhile Investment

The word Nishkam means selfless — acting without desire for personal reward. It comes from the Sikh tradition of Nishkam Seva, selfless service, but its meaning speaks across all faiths and all people.

When Guru Nanak Dev Ji was a young man, his father Mehta Kalu Ji gave him money to go to the market and make a good trade. On the way, Guru Nanak and his companion Bhai Bala Ji came across a group of hungry holy men — sadhus — who had not eaten for many days. Guru Nanak used the money to buy food and clothing for them. When he returned home, his father was angry. But Guru Nanak gently explained that he had made sacha sauda — a true and worthwhile investment. The profit was not financial. The return was human goodness.

Today, Gurdwara Sacha Sauda stands at the site where Guru Nanak fed the hungry. The town of Chuharkana is now known as Farooqabad, and remains a busy market city — a reminder that the most valuable trade is the one that serves others.

This is the lens through which we study business at Nishkam School West London. Yes, we learn about revenue, profit margins and market share. But we also ask: what is a business for? Is it only to make money — or is it to serve people, create livelihoods, treat workers with dignity, and leave the world better than we found it?

The Udasi Journeys

Guru Nanak Dev Ji travelled across the known world for almost 25 years — four great journeys known as the Udasi. He crossed deserts, mountains and oceans. He visited places sacred to Hindus, Muslims, Buddhists and Jains. In every place, he asked the same question: are you living truthfully?

In Punjabi, each of Guru Nanak's journeys is called an udasi — a word that suggests stepping back from the world to see it more clearly. When you step back and look at a business case study in your exam, you are doing something similar: observing with a clear mind, analysing what is really happening, and making a judgement based on evidence rather than assumption.

The question Guru Nanak asked in every place he visited is the same question at the heart of every 12-mark answer you will ever write: what is the right thing to do — and why?

Dharam-Yudh — The True Inner Battle

The Sikh concept of Dharam-yudh describes the inner battle every person faces: the struggle between virtues like love, truth, compassion, humility and contentment on one side, and the five thieves — anger, ego, greed, attachment and lust — on the other.

In Business Studies, this inner battle plays out in every ethical decision. Should a business cut costs by using cheaper suppliers with poor working conditions? Should a company prioritise short-term profit over long-term sustainability? When you evaluate these decisions in your exam, you are engaging in your own dharam-yudh — weighing what is profitable against what is right.

The Sikh principle of Kirat — honest, hard work — and Vand Chakko — sharing what you earn with others — are not just religious teachings. They are the foundation of every ethical business you will study in this course.

Pillar I

Faith-Inspired

Learning from the wisdom of all traditions. Businesses that act with integrity and compassion reflect values at the heart of every great faith. In Business Studies, we connect every topic to these deeper principles.

Pillar II

Virtues-Led

Fostering compassion, humility, justice and courage. Every business decision is an ethical choice — a chance to practise virtue or fall short of it. Our 45 core virtues guide how we learn, how we treat each other, and how we approach our work.

Becoming the best human beings we can possibly be. In Business Studies, this means thinking critically, writing with precision, and approaching every question with diligence and determination.

Our Four Form Group Virtues

Every year group at Nishkam School West London is organised into four form groups, each named after a virtue that shapes how we live and learn together. These are not just names — they are qualities we practise every day.

Compassion

Form Group Virtue

“Those who know their Lord and Master, show compassion; then, they become immortal, and attain the state of eternal dignity.” — Sri Guru Granth Sahib, p. 340

Compassion is understanding and caring for someone who is in trouble, is hurt, or has made a mistake. In Business Studies, compassion means considering the impact of decisions on all stakeholders — not just shareholders. When a business chooses to pay a living wage or source materials ethically, it is choosing compassion over convenience.

Contentment

Form Group Virtue

“To be content doesn’t mean you don’t desire more, it means you’re thankful for what you have and patient for what’s to come.”

Contentment is an awareness of sufficiency — a sense that we have enough and we are enough. In Business Studies, contentment teaches us that growth for growth's sake is not always wise. A business that expands too fast without solid foundations may collapse. Sustainable, patient growth is a sign of wisdom, not weakness.

Humility

Form Group Virtue

“True humility is not thinking less of yourself; it is thinking of yourself less.” — C. S. Lewis

When you are humble, you don't consider yourself more important than other people. You are happy to serve others. In Business Studies, humility means acknowledging what you don't know and being willing to learn from evidence. The best business leaders listen to their employees and customers. Our motto says it all: Man neeva, mat uchi — to be humble, to be wise.

Truth

Form Group Virtue

“Three things cannot be long hidden: the sun, the moon, and the truth.” — Buddha

Truthfulness means your words and actions are full of truth. You live by your own true nature. In Business Studies, truth means being honest in your analysis — write what the evidence shows, not what you think the examiner wants to hear. When a business publishes transparent financial reports or admits a product recall, it is practising truth.

Our Three Classroom Virtues

In every Business Studies lesson, three virtues guide how we learn, how we work, and how we approach challenges. These are the virtues you will see embedded in every unit of this course.

Peacefulness

In Every Lesson

Approach complex questions with a calm, focused mind. Listen to different stakeholder perspectives with respect. Create a learning environment where every voice is valued. In the exam, peacefulness means staying composed under pressure — if you get stuck, move on and come back.

Diligence

In Every Lesson

Commit fully to mastering each topic. Check your chains of analysis, refine your evaluations, and never submit work that is less than your best. Show your working on every calculation. Read every case study twice. Diligence is the difference between a student who knows the content and a student who earns top marks.

Determination

In Every Lesson

Persevere through challenging 9 and 12-mark questions. Treat every mistake as a stepping stone. Keep pushing toward your target grade and beyond. In the exam, determination means never leaving a question blank — even a partial answer can earn marks. The students who keep writing are the ones who reach the highest levels.
