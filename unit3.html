Production Processes

Spec ref: 3.3.1 · Tested every year — job vs flow MCQs and 6/9/12-mark lean production questions

What Is Production?

Production is the process of converting inputs (raw materials, labour, capital) into outputs (finished goods or services). The method a business chooses depends on the nature of the product, the size of the order and the budget available.

Key Definitions
  • Productivity: the amount of output produced per unit of input (usually per worker per hour).
  • Efficiency: producing the maximum output with the minimum input — less waste, lower unit cost.
  • Unit cost: the cost of producing one item. Falls when productivity rises.

The Three Methods of Production

MethodWhat It IsExamplesTypical Business
Job ProductionOne product is made at a time, to a customer's individual specification. High skill, high price, low volume.Wedding cakes, bespoke suits, custom furniture, architect-designed houses.Small specialist business, sole trader or craftsman.
Batch ProductionGroups of identical items produced together, then the production line is reset to produce a different batch.Bakery making 100 loaves, then 100 croissants. Clothing manufacturer making one size then the next.Medium-sized business — bakery, brewery, clothing factory.
Flow ProductionIdentical products made continuously on a production line. High volume, high automation, low unit cost.Cars on an assembly line, bottled drinks, smartphones, breakfast cereals.Large manufacturer — Toyota, Coca-Cola, Unilever.

Job Production — Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Products can be fully customised to each customer's exact needs.
  • Higher prices can be charged — bespoke goods carry premium value.
  • Highly motivated skilled workers — variety keeps work interesting.
  • Flexible — easy to switch between different orders.

Disadvantages

  • Very high labour cost — skilled workers are expensive.
  • Slow — one product at a time means low output per day.
  • Cannot benefit from economies of scale.
  • High unit cost — the finished product is expensive to produce.

Flow Production — Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Very high output — produces huge quantities quickly.
  • Low unit cost through economies of scale.
  • Can use lower-skilled workers or automation — less training needed.
  • Consistent quality — every unit is identical.

Disadvantages

  • Very high set-up costs — machinery and factory are expensive.
  • No customisation — every product is identical.
  • Workers can become demotivated by repetitive tasks.
  • A breakdown halts the whole line — costly in lost output.

Lean Production

Lean production is a philosophy pioneered by Toyota. The aim is to eliminate waste from the production process — waste of time, materials, effort, stock or space.

The Three Pillars of Lean
  • Just-in-Time (JIT): stock is ordered and delivered only when needed for production — no warehouse full of raw materials.
  • Kaizen (continuous improvement): every worker is empowered to suggest small daily improvements to the process.
  • Waste reduction: cutting waste in materials, movement, waiting time, defects and overproduction.

Benefits of Lean Production

  • Lower storage costs — less stock to keep safe.
  • Less money tied up in stock — better cash flow.
  • Less waste of materials — lower unit cost.
  • Higher quality as every worker takes responsibility.
  • Motivated workforce — employees feel trusted and involved.

Risks of Lean Production

  • If a supplier fails, production halts immediately.
  • Requires strong, reliable supplier relationships.
  • High training cost to build a Kaizen culture.
  • Struggles to respond to sudden demand spikes with no stock buffer.
  • Vulnerable to disruption — weather, strikes, global shocks (e.g. COVID).

Technology in Production

TechnologyWhat It DoesImpact on Operations
CAD (Computer-Aided Design)Engineers and designers use software to design products digitally before they are made.Faster design cycle; easier to modify; fewer mistakes at prototype stage.
CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacture)Computer-controlled machinery manufactures the product automatically from the CAD design.Greater precision; consistent quality; works 24/7; reduces human error.
Robotics & AutomationProgrammable robots perform repetitive tasks on the production line.Higher output; lower long-term labour cost; safer for dangerous tasks.
AI & Data AnalyticsAI predicts demand, optimises stock levels and schedules production.Less waste; better planning; fewer stock-outs; competitive advantage.
Link to 9/12-Mark Analysis

When evaluating technology, always bring in four chains: high initial cost → higher productivity → lower unit cost → competitive pricing → more revenue → higher profit. Then balance with the risk of redundancies, employee demotivation or technology failure.

Case Study — Toyota's Production System

Real-World Example
Toyota Production System (TPS)

Toyota invented lean production after World War II. Japan had very little money, land or raw materials, so Toyota had to make cars without wasting anything. TPS uses JIT, Kaizen and total quality thinking. Today, Toyota produces around 10 million cars a year with significantly lower waste and higher productivity than most competitors. Every worker can stop the production line by pulling an andon cord if they spot a defect — because fixing the problem at source costs far less than fixing it later.

MCQs — Production Processes (Past Papers)

2025 P1 · Q1.11 mark

Which of the following is an advantage of job production?

  • A Allows a business to achieve economies of scale
  • B Allows for specialisation of workers
  • C Allows large quantities to be produced
  • D Allows the customisation of products
Why D is correctJob production makes ONE product at a time to a customer's exact specification. Economies of scale (A) and large quantities (C) come from flow production. Specialisation (B) appears more in flow than job.
The word "custom" is the giveaway. If the question asks about customisation or bespoke work — think JOB production, not flow.
2025 P1 · Q1.61 mark

Which of the following describes the lean production technique of just-in-time (JIT)?

  • A Where a product moves continuously from one stage of the process to another
  • B Where the business buys raw materials in bulk before orders are received
  • C Where the business only produces the product when a customer places an order
  • D Where the business holds large amounts of stock to meet customer demand
Why C is correctJIT is pull-production: the customer order triggers production. A describes flow production; B and D describe Just-in-Case (JIC) — the opposite of JIT.
B and D both describe JIC, not JIT. Remember: JIT = lean, no stock buffer. JIC = safe, large stock buffer.
Examiner Traps — Production
  • Job production cannot benefit from economies of scale — it is too slow.
  • Flow production does NOT allow customisation. Custom = job.
  • JIT = "just in time" = no stock buffer. JIC = "just in case" = bulk stock held.
  • Lean does not just mean "fast" — it means eliminating waste. Read the exact wording.

The Role of Procurement

Spec ref: 3.3.2 · JIT vs JIC tested annually; supplier choice is a high-frequency 6-mark topic

What Is Procurement?

Procurement is the process of finding, agreeing terms with, and buying goods or services from suppliers. It covers everything from sourcing raw materials to signing delivery contracts. Good procurement keeps costs down, maintains quality and ensures reliable supply.

Key Definitions
  • Supplier: a business that provides raw materials, components or services to another business.
  • Stock (inventory): raw materials, work-in-progress or finished goods held by a business.
  • Lead time: the time between placing an order and receiving the stock.
  • Supply chain: the network of suppliers, manufacturers, warehouses and retailers that get a product to the customer.

Choosing a Supplier — The Key Factors

FactorWhy It MattersRisk If Ignored
PriceDirectly affects the cost of sales and the profit margin on every unit sold.Overpaying erodes profit; underpaying may mean low quality.
QualityPoor raw materials mean poor finished products — affects brand reputation and returns.Customer complaints, lost repeat business, damaged brand.
ReliabilityA supplier who delivers late halts production and disappoints customers.Production stopped; lost sales; reputation damaged.
LocationCloser suppliers mean shorter lead times, lower transport costs and a smaller carbon footprint.Longer delays, higher transport cost, less flexibility.
Ethics & SustainabilityCustomers increasingly care about Fairtrade, fair wages and environmental impact.Reputational damage if exposed using unethical suppliers.
Payment TermsLonger credit (e.g. 60 days) improves cash flow.Cash flow squeeze if supplier demands payment on delivery.

Just-in-Time (JIT) vs Just-in-Case (JIC)

FeatureJust-in-Time (JIT)Just-in-Case (JIC)
Stock heldMinimal / zero buffer stockLarge safety buffer held in warehouses
Storage costVery lowHigh — rent, insurance, security
Cash flowStronger — less money tied up in stockWeaker — capital locked in stock
Risk of stock-outHigh — any delay halts productionLow — the buffer protects against delays
Supplier relationshipMust be very close, very reliableLess critical — stock provides cushion
Best suited toStable demand, reliable suppliers (e.g. Toyota)Unpredictable demand or unreliable supply

The Impact of Logistics & Supply Chain

Logistics is the management of the movement of goods — from supplier to factory to customer. An efficient supply chain means:

Benefits of an Efficient Supply Chain
  • Lower costs: bulk ordering, fewer deliveries, lower transport cost.
  • Faster delivery: happier customers, competitive advantage.
  • Less waste: no products damaged or expired in transit.
  • Better data: tracking every delivery gives the business real-time information.
  • Resilience: having multiple suppliers reduces the risk if one fails.
Recent Real-World Disruptions
  • COVID-19 (2020–22) exposed the risks of JIT — factories worldwide ran out of parts.
  • The Suez Canal blockage (2021) delayed global shipping for weeks.
  • Brexit (2021 onwards) added customs delays and paperwork for UK-EU trade.
  • The Red Sea shipping disruption (2023–24) pushed up transport costs for thousands of UK businesses.

Case Study — Primark's Supply Chain

Real-World Example
Primark — the ultra-cheap retailer

Primark sells clothing at prices far below competitors like H&M or Zara. Its procurement strategy is built on: very large bulk orders (lowering unit cost dramatically), long-standing supplier relationships in Bangladesh, India and China, and no online sales — which keeps logistics simple. However, Primark has faced repeated ethical criticism after factory collapses (Rana Plaza, 2013) exposed poor working conditions at some suppliers. The company has since invested heavily in supplier audits — an example of how ethical procurement has become a central business issue, not just a PR one.

The Concept of Quality

Spec ref: 3.3.3 · TQM almost every year; consequences of poor quality tested in 4-mark and 6-mark questions

What Is Quality?

Quality is about meeting or exceeding customer expectations. It does not necessarily mean expensive — a ninety-nine pence Primark T-shirt can be "good quality" if it does the job the customer expected for the price paid. Quality is fitness for purpose.

Key Definitions
  • Quality Control (QC): checking the finished product at the end of production for defects. Inspection-based.
  • Quality Assurance (QA): building quality into every stage of the process so defects are prevented, not just caught.
  • Total Quality Management (TQM): a company-wide philosophy where every employee is responsible for quality, aiming for zero defects.

Quality Control vs Quality Assurance vs TQM

ApproachWhen Quality Is CheckedWho Is ResponsibleMindset
Quality ControlAt the end of productionSeparate QC inspectors"Catch defects before they leave the factory."
Quality AssuranceAt every stage of productionEach department/team"Prevent defects happening in the first place."
Total Quality ManagementContinuously, everywhereEvery single employee"Quality is everyone's job — always."

Why Quality Matters

Benefits of High Quality

  • Strong brand reputation — customers trust the product.
  • Higher prices can be charged — customers pay for quality.
  • Repeat business and customer loyalty.
  • Free word-of-mouth advertising (reviews, recommendations).
  • Fewer returns, refunds and complaints — lower cost.
  • Competitive advantage over cheaper, lower-quality rivals.

Consequences of Poor Quality

  • Customer complaints and negative online reviews.
  • Product recalls — very expensive and damaging.
  • Lost repeat business — customers go to competitors.
  • Falling sales and falling market share.
  • Legal action for unsafe products.
  • Reputation damage that can take years to repair.

The Cost of Quality

Improving quality costs money in the short term — staff training, better materials, better machinery. But the cost of poor quality is far higher: refunds, lost customers, damaged brand, legal claims.

Quality EquationCost of Quality < Cost of Poor Quality

Investing in quality up front is nearly always cheaper than fixing problems later.

Case Studies — Quality in Action

Real-World Example
Boeing 737 MAX — the cost of poor quality

In 2018 and 2019 two Boeing 737 MAX aircraft crashed, killing 346 people. Investigators found that a software system had been poorly designed and inadequately tested. The aircraft was grounded worldwide for 20 months. Boeing lost over $20 billion in compensation, legal costs and lost orders. It is a stark reminder that when quality fails in a safety-critical product, the consequences are catastrophic — human and financial.

Real-World Example
Rolls-Royce — quality as brand

Rolls-Royce cars sell for upwards of £300,000 each. Every car is hand-built over hundreds of hours, with individual craftsmen signing off each stage. The business makes far fewer cars than Toyota — but earns enormous profit per unit because the brand stands for uncompromising quality. Quality is not just an operations choice; it is the entire business model.

Good Customer Service

Spec ref: 3.3.4 · Customer service tested every year — methods, benefits and dangers of poor service

What Is Customer Service?

Customer service is the experience the customer has before, during and after buying a product. It covers everything from the friendliness of a cashier to the speed of a delivery to how fairly a complaint is handled. Good service turns first-time buyers into loyal, repeat customers.

Why Customer Service Matters More Than Ever
  • One unhappy customer tells roughly 10 people. A happy customer tells 3.
  • Online reviews (Trustpilot, Google, Tripadvisor) make poor service instantly visible to thousands.
  • Winning a new customer costs up to 5 times more than keeping an existing one.
  • Repeat customers typically spend more per visit than new ones.

Methods of Delivering Good Customer Service

MethodWhat It InvolvesExample
Product KnowledgeStaff know the product inside out — features, benefits, uses, availability.Apple Store staff trained to answer detailed technical questions.
Customer EngagementStaff greet, listen, suggest and follow up — making the customer feel valued.John Lewis partners offering personal styling advice.
Speed of ResponseFast answers to queries, quick deliveries, short waiting times.Amazon next-day Prime delivery, live chat within 30 seconds.
Post-Sales ServiceWarranties, returns, after-care, follow-up emails.AO.com's no-quibble 30-day returns and recycling pickup.
Complaint HandlingQuick, fair resolution — the customer leaves happier than they arrived.Nationwide's "we pay your phone bill" if their service lets you down.
Digital Self-ServiceEasy-to-use app, website, chatbot or FAQ.Monzo banking app — manage everything without calling.

Benefits of Good Customer Service

Benefits to the Business

  • Repeat custom — existing customers return again and again.
  • Word-of-mouth advertising (and five-star reviews).
  • Customers willing to pay higher prices for better experience.
  • Competitive advantage over rivals offering identical products.
  • Higher motivation and lower staff turnover.

Dangers of Poor Customer Service

  • Lost customers who defect to competitors.
  • One-star reviews that deter new buyers for years.
  • Viral social media complaints that damage the brand overnight.
  • Falling sales and falling profit.
  • Demoralised staff who face angry customers every day.

The Role of ICT in Modern Customer Service

TechnologyImpact
CRM (Customer Relationship Management)Software that tracks every customer interaction — purchases, complaints, preferences — so staff can personalise service.
Chatbots & AIHandle routine queries 24/7 — releasing human staff for complex cases.
Review PlatformsTripadvisor, Trustpilot, Google Reviews — every customer becomes a public reviewer.
Social MediaCustomers now expect to tweet a complaint and get a reply within minutes.
Mobile AppsSelf-service for bookings, tracking, complaints — faster and cheaper.
Nishkam Lens — Service as Seva

Customer service is commercial Seva — selfless service made visible. Every interaction is a chance to treat another human being with dignity. The best businesses understand that quality of service is not a cost — it is a core expression of who they are.

Case Study — Zappos (USA)

Real-World Example
Zappos — the customer service online retailer

Zappos sells shoes online. Its most famous call lasted over ten hours, with a representative helping a customer decide on a single pair. The company offers free returns for 365 days and has a culture where every employee receives four weeks of customer service training regardless of their role. Zappos proved that treating customers with extraordinary care is not a cost centre — it is a growth strategy. The company was acquired by Amazon for $1.2 billion in 2009 largely because of its customer service reputation.

9-Mark Exemplar: Should GB Ltd switch from JIC to JIT?

Case Study — GB Ltd (practice item)

GB Ltd manufactures bicycles in the UK. Currently it holds six weeks of raw material stock as a safety buffer. Its main supplier is based 400 miles away. Storage costs are £180,000 per year. Two competitors have recently moved to JIT and cut their unit cost by 12%. GB Ltd's managing director is considering the same move.

Model 9-Mark Answer

Q: Recommend whether GB Ltd should switch from JIC to JIT stock control. Give reasons for your recommendation. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR switching to JIT (4 chains + data):
An advantage of switching to JIT is that GB Ltd could dramatically reduce its storage costs. Currently GB Ltd spends £180,000 per year on storage. This is because JIT holds almost no stock — raw materials arrive just before they are needed for production, eliminating the need for large warehouses, security and insurance. This means that GB Ltd could cut the £180,000 storage bill substantially, freeing up significant working capital for other uses such as investing in better machinery or staff training. As a result, the unit cost of each bicycle would fall — matching the 12% unit cost reduction that two competitors have already achieved — allowing GB Ltd to lower prices or widen profit margins. Therefore, switching to JIT could have the biggest impact on GB Ltd's competitiveness in a market where rivals are already cutting costs.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view would recognise the significant risks of JIT for GB Ltd specifically. The main supplier is based 400 miles away, which means lead times are already long. This suggests that any disruption — bad weather, strike, a lorry breakdown — would halt GB Ltd's production entirely, because there is no stock buffer to keep the line running. A further limitation is that JIT requires a very close, very reliable relationship with suppliers, and building this takes time and trust — GB Ltd may not be able to switch quickly. It also depends on whether GB Ltd's demand is stable; if orders fluctuate seasonally, JIT makes it much harder to respond to sudden spikes.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe GB Ltd should switch to JIT — but only after finding a second, closer supplier to reduce the risk of delivery failures. Although the 400-mile distance from its main supplier creates real risk, this is outweighed by the competitive pressure: two competitors have already cut unit costs by 12%, meaning GB Ltd faces losing market share if it does nothing. However, this depends on whether a secondary UK-based supplier can be secured to provide a backup in case the main supplier fails. The most important factor is that the bicycle market is highly price-competitive; a 12% unit cost gap is a very significant disadvantage, and GB Ltd cannot ignore it without losing sales. In the short term the switch will be risky and require careful supplier negotiation, but in the long term the storage savings and competitive pricing will protect GB Ltd's market position. Therefore, GB Ltd should switch to JIT but invest in supply chain resilience at the same time.

12-Mark Exemplar: Quality Assurance or Total Quality Management?

Case Study — Rivera Foods Ltd (practice item)
Rivera Foods Ltd

Rivera Foods Ltd produces ready-meals. Last year it had 42 customer complaints about foreign objects in its meals — double the previous year. Profit fell 8% as a result of refunds and lost customers. The directors are choosing between two options.
Option 1 — Quality Assurance (QA): Introduce checks at every stage of production. Estimated cost £60,000 per year. Would reduce defects by around 70%.
Option 2 — Total Quality Management (TQM): Retrain every employee, create a company-wide culture of zero defects. Estimated cost £140,000 in year one, then £50,000 a year. Competitors who use TQM report near-zero defect rates within 18 months.

Model 12-Mark Answer

Q: Evaluate which of these two options will have the biggest impact on Rivera Foods Ltd's quality and profitability. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Quality Assurance (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of adopting Quality Assurance is that Rivera Foods Ltd could reduce defects quickly with a modest investment. QA would cost £60,000 per year and reduce defects by around 70%. This is because introducing checks at every stage of production catches problems before they reach the finished product, preventing foreign objects from ending up in a customer's meal. This means that the 42 complaints recorded last year would fall dramatically, refund costs would drop, and customer trust would begin to recover. As a result, the 8% profit fall caused by poor quality could be reversed within a year — with the £60,000 cost paid back many times over by retained customers. Therefore, QA could have the biggest impact on the immediate problem of rising complaints. However, this depends on whether inspectors are rigorous enough to catch every defect, or whether defects still slip through because workers themselves do not feel ownership of quality.

Evaluation — Option 1:
A more balanced assessment is that QA treats quality as an inspection problem rather than a cultural one — workers may continue to produce defects knowing that "someone else" will catch them. This is significant because Rivera Foods Ltd's complaints have doubled in a year, suggesting a deeper culture problem that inspections alone may not fix.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: Total Quality Management (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of adopting TQM is that Rivera Foods Ltd could achieve near-zero defect rates, eliminating the quality problem at source. Competitors using TQM report near-zero defect rates within 18 months. This is because TQM makes every single employee responsible for quality, so defects are prevented at the moment they would occur rather than caught after the fact. This means that the cost of refunds, lost customers and reputational damage would disappear almost entirely — not just fall by 70%. As a result, Rivera Foods Ltd would rebuild its brand reputation and could potentially charge a small premium for consistently high-quality ready-meals, increasing revenue as well as reducing cost. Therefore, TQM could have the biggest impact on both defect rates and long-term profitability. However, this depends on whether Rivera Foods Ltd can sustain the cultural change for the full 18 months needed — if managers revert to old habits, the investment is wasted.

Evaluation — Option 2:
A limitation is that TQM costs £140,000 in year one — more than twice the cost of QA. It also depends on whether Rivera Foods Ltd can afford this upfront investment while profit is already falling. By contrast, QA offers a much cheaper, faster solution — though one that does not fully address the underlying culture problem.

Conclusion — 5 Steps:
To conclude, I think that TQM will have the biggest impact on Rivera Foods Ltd's quality and profitability. Although QA is cheaper in the short term and would reduce defects by 70%, this is outweighed by the fact that TQM addresses the root cause of the problem: complaints have doubled in a year, suggesting the whole production culture needs to change, not just its inspection process. However, this depends on whether Rivera Foods Ltd can secure finance for the £140,000 upfront cost — possibly by using the £60,000 it would have spent on QA and borrowing the rest against the business's future TQM-driven profit growth. The most important factor is that in the ready-meals market, food safety is non-negotiable — a serious incident could trigger a product recall costing far more than either option. TQM is the only approach that gets close to zero defects, which is the only acceptable standard in food manufacturing. In the short term QA looks cheaper and faster, but in the long term TQM protects the brand and builds a culture that makes another doubling of complaints far less likely. Therefore, TQM is the best option because it addresses the cultural root of Rivera Foods Ltd's quality problem rather than just its symptoms.

Exam Technique Masterclass

How to answer every question type for maximum marks
1–2Marks — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase, or single sentence. No explanation needed.
  • Read ALL four options before selecting (MCQs).
  • Use elimination for MCQs.
  • Never write more than one sentence for 1 mark.
2Marks — Explain
Point + development. Two clear sentences. ONE chain of analysis.
  • Rule: marks minus one = number of chains.
  • State the point, then develop using a connective.
4Marks — Explain with context
Point → Explain → Impact. THREE chains of analysis.
  • MUST use case study data where given.
  • Link impact back to the business in the question.
6Marks — Analyse
Point → Evidence → Explain with THREE chains → Link to outcome
  • Use case study evidence in blue.
  • End with a clear outcome for the business.
From 2026, "analyse one reason" now requires TWO developed reasons/points.
9Marks — Recommend
THREE paragraphs: (1) Argument with 4 chains + data manipulation, (2) Purely evaluative — "more balanced view / further limitation / depends on", (3) Conclusion: "Overall, I believe" + NEW information + short/long term + "Therefore".
  • Para 1: Point → Evidence from case study → Data manipulation → Consequence → Therefore. Minimum 4 chain connectors.
  • Para 2: Purely evaluative — weigh risks and limitations. Use "a more balanced view", "a further limitation", "it also depends on".
  • Para 3: Clear recommendation with NEW information not in paragraphs 1 or 2. Must include short-term vs long-term reasoning. No fence-sitting.
  • Conclusion that only repeats paragraph 1 = Level 2 maximum.
12Marks — Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 3: Conclusion with 5 steps.
  • BOTH options: 4 chains each. Manipulate data (calculate savings, percentages, compare figures).
  • Each option followed by a short concise evaluation (2–3 sentences).
  • Conclusion 5 steps: (1) "To conclude…" (2) "Although… this is outweighed by…" (3) "However, this depends on…" (4) "The most important factor" + NEW information (5) "Therefore…"
  • NEW information MUST appear in the conclusion — a factor, calculation or stakeholder point not raised in paragraphs 1 or 2.
  • Integrate functional areas — link costs → pricing → competitiveness → revenue → profit. Isolated analysis = Level 3 maximum.

Key Terms Glossary — Unit 3

Every definition you need to know for the exam
Production
The process of converting inputs (materials, labour, capital) into finished outputs (goods or services).
Productivity
The amount of output produced per unit of input (often measured per worker per hour).
Efficiency
Producing the maximum output with the minimum input — minimising waste and unit cost.
Job Production
Making one product at a time to a customer's individual specification. High skill, high price, low volume.
Batch Production
Producing groups of identical products together, then resetting to produce another batch.
Flow Production
Continuous mass production of identical products on an assembly line. High volume, low unit cost.
Lean Production
A philosophy (pioneered by Toyota) of eliminating all forms of waste in the production process.
Just-in-Time (JIT)
Stock is ordered and delivered only when needed for production — no buffer stock held.
Just-in-Case (JIC)
A safety buffer of stock is held in case of supplier delays or demand spikes.
Kaizen
The philosophy of continuous improvement — small daily improvements suggested by every employee.
CAD
Computer-Aided Design — software used by designers and engineers to create digital product designs.
CAM
Computer-Aided Manufacture — computer-controlled machinery that produces goods from a digital design.
Automation
Using machinery and software to perform tasks previously done by human workers.
Procurement
The process of finding, agreeing terms with, and buying from suppliers.
Supplier
A business that provides raw materials, components or services to another business.
Lead Time
The time between placing an order with a supplier and receiving the stock.
Supply Chain
The network of suppliers, manufacturers, warehouses and retailers that move a product from raw material to customer.
Logistics
The management of the movement and storage of goods along the supply chain.
Quality
Meeting or exceeding customer expectations — fitness for purpose at the price paid.
Quality Control (QC)
Inspection-based approach: checking the finished product at the end of production for defects.
Quality Assurance (QA)
Building quality into every stage of the process so defects are prevented rather than caught.
Total Quality Management (TQM)
A company-wide philosophy where every employee is responsible for achieving zero defects.
Customer Service
The experience a customer has before, during and after purchase — covers speed, product knowledge, attitude, and after-care.
CRM
Customer Relationship Management — software that tracks every customer interaction to personalise service.
Product Recall
When a business withdraws a product from sale because of a fault — usually very costly.
Customer Loyalty
Repeat business from satisfied customers — usually the most profitable source of revenue.

The virtues Nishkam builds in you — integrity, compassion, diligence, humility — are exactly what AI cannot replicate.
