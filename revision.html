Planning · AQA GCSE Business Studies

How to
revise.

A strategic, phased revision guide. Exam technique for every mark tariff, AO2 mastery drills, examiner traps and a 7-day final-week plan.

PHASE 1

Know the Content

Foundation — start here, no matter when your exam is

Before you can answer any exam question, you must know the content. Not vaguely — precisely. AQA will test whether you know exact definitions, can compare concepts, and understand how business areas connect to each other.

The RAG Audit Go through every topic in each unit and rate yourself honestly: Red = I don't know this, Amber = I sort of know this, Green = I could explain this to someone else without notes. Revise Red topics first. Don't skip this step — most students overestimate what they know.
How to Learn Definitions Learn 2-3 definitions per session using flashcards or the Quiz tool. Say definitions out loud — hearing yourself significantly helps retention. Test yourself after 1 day, then 3 days, then a week (spaced repetition). If you can write the definition from memory without checking, it's in your long-term memory.
Examiner Trap: Reading is NOT Revising Re-reading your notes feels productive but research shows it is one of the least effective revision methods. Your brain tricks you into thinking "I recognise this, so I know it." Instead, close your notes and write everything you know about a topic from memory (brain dump). Then check what you missed. The gaps are what you need to revise.

Use the Exam Mastery Notes on this site as your content source. Each unit page has comprehensive notes, definitions in info boxes, advantages/disadvantages tables, and real-world examples — everything you need to know for the exam.

PHASE 2

Exam Technique — Question by Question

The marks are in the method, not just the knowledge

Knowing the content gets you halfway. The other half is knowing exactly what the examiner wants for each mark tariff. Every mark tariff has a formula. Learn the formula, apply it every time, and you will consistently hit the top of each level.

1
State / Identify / Which of the following
Time: 30 seconds

One word, phrase, or sentence. No explanation needed. For MCQs, read all four options before selecting — eliminate obviously wrong answers first.

TrapDon't waste time explaining your MCQ choice. There is no space and no marks for it. Tick and move on.
2
Explain / Identify two
Time: 2 minutes

For "Identify two": just name two things — no development needed. For "Explain one": state the point, then develop it using a connector.

Point. This means that... + development.
Trap: The "Two Halves" ErrorStudents write "shareholders and owners" as two different stakeholders. They are the same thing. Similarly, "aims and objectives" cannot be listed as two separate parts of a business plan. Read your two answers together — if they're really the same point in different words, you'll only get 1 mark.
4
Explain with Case Study
Time: 5 minutes

State a point, build a chain of 3 connectors, and weave in case study data (the business name, specific figures, details from the item).

Point. [Case study data — business name, figures, specifics] This means that... As a result... Therefore...

Rule: marks minus 1 = chains needed. 4 marks = 3 chains of analysis.

Trap: Generic AnswersThe most common reason students get 2/4 instead of 4/4 is that their answer could apply to any business. Use the business name. Quote specific data from the item. Mention their products, location, staff numbers, financial figures. If your answer works without the case study, it's too generic.
6
Analyse TWO (2026 format)
Time: 10 minutes
2026 ChangeFrom 2026, 6-mark questions now say "Analyse two impacts/ways/benefits." You must write two separate paragraphs, each with its own developed point and case study data. One point only = Level 2 maximum (4/6). Both points detailed with context = Level 3 (5-6/6).
Paragraph 1: [Open with case study context] Point 1. This means that... As a result... Therefore... [Close with case study link]

Paragraph 2: [Case study context] Point 2 (distinctly different from Point 1). This means that... As a result... Therefore... [Close with case study link]
Trap: Making Both Points Too SimilarAQA says both points need to be "distinctly different." If you write about "lower costs" in paragraph 1 and "saving money" in paragraph 2, that's the same point twice. You'll get Level 2. Make sure each paragraph analyses a genuinely different impact — e.g. one about costs, one about customer satisfaction.
Trap: Adding Evaluation to a 6-Mark Answer6-mark questions do NOT require evaluation. There is no green (evaluation) in a 6-marker. Students who write "However, on the other hand..." are wasting time that should be spent developing their second analytical point. Save evaluation for 9-mark and 12-mark questions only.
9
Recommend / Evaluate
Time: 14 minutes

Three paragraphs. A clear structure that builds an argument, weighs it up, then reaches a justified conclusion.

Para 1 — Argument (4 chains + case study data manipulation):
Point. [Data from case study — use the numbers] This means that... As a result... Therefore... Which leads to...

Para 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative, no new argument):
However, a more balanced view would suggest... A further limitation is... It also depends on...

Para 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe... + [NEW information not mentioned in Para 1 or 2] + In the short term... but in the long term... Therefore...
Trap: The Fence-Sitting Conclusion"It depends on many factors and could go either way." This is a Level 2 conclusion at best. You MUST commit to a position. "Overall, I recommend..." or "I believe the most important factor is..." The examiner wants a clear judgement, not a summary of what you've already said.
Trap: Conclusion Repeats Para 1Your conclusion must add NEW information. If you find yourself writing the same points again, stop. Introduce a new factor — something from the case study you haven't used yet, a short-term vs long-term distinction, or a condition that would change your recommendation.
12
Analyse + Evaluate TWO Options
Time: 22 minutes

The highest-value question on each paper. Worth 13% of your total marks. This is where grade boundaries are won and lost.

Para 1 — Option 1 (4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on"):
Analyse Option 1 in detail. [Use specific figures from case study] This means that... As a result... Therefore...
Short evaluation: "However, this depends on..."

Para 2 — Option 2 (4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on"):
Analyse Option 2 in detail. Mirror the structure of Para 1.
Short evaluation: "However, this depends on..."

Conclusion (5 steps):
1. "To conclude, I believe Option X will have the biggest impact on [stem]..."
2. "Although Option Y offers [benefit], this is outweighed by..."
3. "However, this depends on..."
4. "The most important factor is..." + [NEW information from case study]
5. "Therefore..."
2026 Change: Evaluation ThroughoutLevel 4 (10-12 marks) now requires "evaluation throughout the response" — not just at the end. This means your "depends on" sentences after each option count as evaluation. You don't need to save all evaluation for the conclusion. Integrate it as you go.
Trap: The Stem is EverythingThe final line of a 12-mark question tells you what to evaluate. "Evaluate which will have the biggest impact on profitability" means your conclusion must be about profitability — not customer satisfaction, not growth, not employee motivation. If you answer a different question to the one the stem asks, you cannot reach Level 4. Read the stem twice before you start writing.
Trap: Isolated Analysis = Level 3 MaximumAQA says: "Interdependent nature of business areas is fully analysed and evaluated." This means you must connect functional areas. If you analyse costs in isolation without linking to pricing, competitiveness, revenue, and ultimately profit, your analysis stays isolated. Always chain through: costs... pricing... competitiveness... revenue... profit.
Trap: No Context in ConclusionA conclusion without case study data cannot reach Level 4. The conclusion must include specific references to the business — its situation, its data, its challenges. Generic conclusions like "Overall this would be good for the business" are Level 2.
PHASE 3

Mastering AO2 — The Skill That Separates Grades

Application to context — the single biggest weakness across all students

AO2 (Application) is worth 30 marks across both papers — and it is consistently the weakest assessment objective in examiners' reports year after year. Students know the theory but fail to apply it to the specific business in the case study. This is the skill that separates a Grade 5 from a Grade 7.

What AO2 Actually Means AO2 = using specific information from the case study to support your answer. This includes: the business name, its products/services, financial data (revenue, costs, profit, percentages), employee numbers, location, ownership type, specific challenges mentioned in the item, and any tables/figures provided. If you delete the business name from your answer and it still makes sense for any business, you have no AO2.

The AO2 Test: Generic vs Applied

GENERIC (NO AO2) — Level 1-2

"Training would help the business because employees would know what to do. This means they would work harder and be more productive. As a result, the business would make more profit."

APPLIED (STRONG AO2) — Level 3

"The two-week training programme at Harper & Lane covers product knowledge and styling advice, which means the 1,200 sales assistants across 85 stores can give customers accurate recommendations. As a result, customer satisfaction improves, which is critical given that store sales have already fallen from £42m to £38m."

GENERIC CONCLUSION — Level 2

"Overall, I think the business should choose Option 1 because it would be cheaper and would help the business grow in the long term."

APPLIED CONCLUSION — Level 4

"To conclude, closing 20 stores saves £4.2m per year, but this is outweighed by the online investment's potential to generate £8.4m additional revenue. The most important factor is that store sales are falling at 8% per year — closing stores manages this decline but does not reverse it. Therefore, only the online investment addresses the root cause."

The Data Manipulation Technique

The highest-scoring students don't just quote data — they manipulate it. This means performing simple calculations on the case study numbers to create new insights the examiner hasn't given you directly.

Example: Data Manipulation in Action The case study tells you: Revenue = £42m (2024), £38m (2025). Costs = £36m (2024), £35m (2025).

Basic AO2 (Level 2): "Revenue has fallen from £42m to £38m."
Manipulated AO2 (Level 3-4): "Profit has fallen from £6m to £3m — a 50% decline — despite costs only falling by £1m. This means revenue is falling faster than costs can be cut, which makes the current trajectory unsustainable."

You calculated the profit figures, the percentage change, and drew a conclusion the case study didn't state explicitly. That is data manipulation — and examiners reward it heavily.
AO2 Practice Drill Pick any case study from a past paper. Before looking at the questions, spend 3 minutes extracting every piece of data: business name, type, products, staff numbers, financial data, challenges, opportunities. Write them in a list. Then attempt the questions — you'll find you naturally weave data into your answers because you've already identified what's available. Do this for 5 different case studies and it becomes automatic.
PHASE 4

Timed Practice Under Exam Conditions

4-6 weeks before the exam — put it all together

You know the content. You know the technique. Now you need to prove you can do both under pressure, in 1 hour 45 minutes, with no notes.

Time Allocation Per Section Total time: 105 minutes for 90 marks. That's roughly 1 minute per mark, plus 10-15 minutes reading time.

Section A (20 marks): 20 minutes — includes reading the front page and all MCQs/short answers
Section B (~33 marks): 35 minutes — read Item A/B carefully, then answer all questions
Section C (~37 marks): 40 minutes — read Item C/D/E carefully, then answer all questions
Buffer: 10 minutes — reading case studies + checking your work
The 3-Minute Case Study Read Before answering any Section B or C question, spend 3 full minutes reading the case study. Underline: the business name, what it does, key financial data, specific problems mentioned, and any data in tables. This investment pays off on every subsequent question — you'll write faster and with better AO2 because you already know what data is available.
Trap: Spending Too Long on the 12-Marker The 12-mark question is worth 13% of your paper. But the six 1-mark MCQs are worth 7%. Some students spend 30+ minutes on the 12-marker and rush the rest. Stick to 22 minutes maximum. A solid Level 3 answer (8-9/12) written in 22 minutes scores more overall than a Level 4 attempt that causes you to lose easy marks elsewhere.

Use the Mock Paper Generator to create fresh full papers. Sit them under strict timed conditions — no phone, no notes, calculator only. Then submit your 9-mark and 12-mark answers to the AI Marker for detailed feedback.

PHASE 5

The Final Week

Days before the exam — consolidate, don't cram

Do NOT attempt to learn new content in the final week. Your job now is to consolidate what you already know, practise retrieval, and build confidence.

Exam Day Checklist Black pen (and a spare). Calculator (check it works). Water bottle. Read the ENTIRE paper before writing. Underline command words. Check the stem of every 9-mark and 12-mark question. Budget your time. Never leave a question blank — even a partial point can earn a mark.
The Nishkam Mindset Peacefulness — approach the exam calmly. If you get stuck, move on and come back. Panic wastes time.
Diligence — show your working on every calculation. Write full chains on every extended answer. Check every MCQ before moving on.
Determination — the 12-mark question is hard for everyone. The students who keep writing, keep chaining, keep applying data — they are the ones who reach Level 4. Do not give up on a question halfway through.

The 10 Most Common Examiner Traps

AQA tests these every single year — learn them and never lose these marks
1. Flat vs Tall StructuresWide span of control = FLAT. Narrow span = TALL. Students reverse this every year. Flat = few layers, wide span, fast communication. Tall = many layers, narrow span, more control.
2. Limited vs Unlimited LiabilityONLY Ltd and Plc have limited liability. Sole traders and partnerships have UNLIMITED liability. This means the owner's personal assets (house, car, savings) can be used to pay business debts.
3. Revenue vs ProfitRevenue = total income from sales. Profit = revenue MINUS costs. A business can have high revenue and still make a loss. Never use "revenue" and "profit" interchangeably.
4. Cash vs ProfitA business can be profitable but run out of cash. Cash flow is about timing — when money comes in and goes out. A profitable business can fail if it can't pay suppliers on time.
5. Primary vs Secondary ResearchInternet research = SECONDARY (existing data you found). Even if you use the internet to send questionnaires, the questionnaire itself is PRIMARY research. Primary = you collected it. Secondary = someone else collected it.
6. Shareholders vs StakeholdersShareholders own shares in the company. Stakeholders include anyone with an interest in the business — employees, customers, suppliers, the community. All shareholders are stakeholders, but not all stakeholders are shareholders.
7. "Analyse" Does NOT Mean "Evaluate"Analyse = develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. Evaluate = make a judgement, weigh up pros/cons, reach a conclusion. If the question says "Analyse", do NOT write "However, on the other hand..." — that's evaluation. Stick to chains.
8. Break-Even — Units, Not MoneyBreak-even output is measured in UNITS, not pounds. Students who write "the break-even point is £50,000" are confusing break-even output with break-even revenue. The formula gives you the number of items you need to sell.
9. Economies of Scale — Unit Cost, Not Total CostEconomies of scale reduce the AVERAGE (unit) cost of production as output increases. Total costs still rise as you produce more — it's the cost PER ITEM that falls. Students who write "economies of scale reduce costs" without specifying "unit costs" lose marks.
10. The 12-Mark Conclusion Must Address the StemThe final sentence of the question (the stem) tells you exactly what to evaluate. "Evaluate which will have the biggest impact on PROFITABILITY" — your conclusion must be about profitability. Not growth. Not customers. Not employees. Profitability. Read the stem. Answer the stem.
