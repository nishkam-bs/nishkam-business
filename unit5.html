Identifying & Understanding Customers

Spec ref: 3.5.1 · Customer needs & wants, market orientation and customer loyalty — tested in Paper 2 MCQs and 4/6-mark questions

Why Customers Come First

Everything in marketing starts with the customer. Every product design, price and advert is an answer to the question: what does the customer value, and what will they pay for? A business that stops listening to its customers stops growing.

Key Definitions
  • Customer: the person who buys the product.
  • Consumer: the person who actually uses the product (sometimes the same person, often not — e.g. a parent buys a toy for a child).
  • Needs: essentials — food, housing, heating, basic clothing.
  • Wants: non-essentials — holidays, gadgets, branded goods.
  • Customer loyalty: repeat buying from the same brand over time.

Market Orientation vs Product Orientation

FeatureProduct OrientationMarket Orientation
Focus"What can we make?""What do customers want?"
Starts withA product idea or inventionMarket research about customers
RiskHigh — product may not match customer needsLower — product built around proven demand
Typical exampleDyson (engineering-led), Apple (design-led)Tesco, Procter & Gamble, Netflix

Identifying Customer Needs & Wants

Good marketing begins with clear answers to a few simple questions:

The Customer Questions
  • Who is the target customer? (age, income, location, lifestyle)
  • What do they need? What problem does our product solve?
  • Why would they buy from us instead of a competitor?
  • When do they buy? (daily, weekly, seasonally, one-off)
  • Where do they shop? (online, in-store, via an app)
  • How much are they willing to pay?

Customer Loyalty & Why It Matters

Benefits of Loyal Customers

  • Repeat revenue — predictable, easier to forecast.
  • Higher average spend — loyal customers typically buy more.
  • Free word-of-mouth marketing (recommendations, reviews).
  • Less sensitive to small price rises.
  • More forgiving of occasional mistakes.

What Drives Customers Away

  • Poor customer service or rude staff.
  • Falling product quality or reliability.
  • Competitors offering the same thing cheaper.
  • Unfair or confusing pricing.
  • A scandal or reputational failure.

Case Study — Aldi in the UK

Real-World Example
Aldi — understanding the price-sensitive customer

Aldi understood something fundamental about UK customers: many shoppers care more about value than brand. Its product range is deliberately narrow (about 2,000 lines vs 30,000+ at Tesco), own-label, and sold from efficient stores with minimal decoration. Aldi's customer base has grown dramatically since the 2008 recession and again during the 2022–23 cost-of-living crisis. Customer understanding — not clever advertising — is the engine of Aldi's growth.

Market Segmentation

Spec ref: 3.5.2 · Segmentation types tested in Paper 2 MCQs and 6-mark extended questions

What Is Segmentation?

Market segmentation means dividing the market into groups of customers with similar characteristics, so the business can tailor its marketing mix to each group. No business can appeal to everyone — smart businesses pick their targets carefully.

Why Segment a Market?
  • Design products that match specific customer needs.
  • Set prices each segment can afford and will pay.
  • Choose the right promotion channels (TV, TikTok, radio).
  • Identify profitable niches competitors are ignoring.
  • Avoid wasting money advertising to people who will never buy.

Types of Segmentation

TypeHow It Divides CustomersExamples
DemographicAge, gender, income, occupation, family size, religionLEGO targets children; Saga targets over-50s; Harrods targets high-income.
GeographicCountry, region, city, urban vs rural, climatePret A Manger mostly in London & big cities; Toolstation across every UK region.
BehaviouralUsage frequency, brand loyalty, reason for buying, benefits soughtNectar/Clubcard segments frequent shoppers; Red Bull segments energy-drink buyers.
Psychographic (Lifestyle)Values, attitudes, personality, lifestyle choicesLululemon segments yoga/wellness lifestyles; The Guardian segments progressive readers.

Advantages & Disadvantages of Segmentation

Advantages

  • Marketing mix can be precisely targeted to each segment.
  • Higher conversion rates — fewer wasted impressions.
  • Opportunity to charge premium prices to high-value segments.
  • Stronger customer loyalty — people feel understood.
  • Spots niche opportunities competitors miss.

Disadvantages

  • Research and analysis costs time and money.
  • Stereotyping risk — people are more complex than their segment.
  • Multiple campaigns cost more than one mass-market campaign.
  • Some segments may be too small to be profitable.
  • Ethical concerns if targeting exploits vulnerable groups.

Case Study — Netflix

Real-World Example
Netflix — hyper-personal segmentation

Netflix segments its customers into hundreds of "taste clusters" based on what each person actually watches, searches, rates and abandons. A thriller fan and a romcom fan opening Netflix on the same evening see completely different home screens. This behavioural segmentation, powered by data and AI, is the reason Netflix's recommendation engine drives around 80% of total viewing hours — and why content budgets can be invested with confidence.

The Purpose & Methods of Market Research

Spec ref: 3.5.3 · Primary vs secondary, quantitative vs qualitative — tested every year

What Is Market Research?

Market research is the process of collecting and analysing information about customers, competitors and the market. It is the foundation of every marketing decision — without it, a business is guessing.

What Research Answers
  • Who are our customers, and what do they value?
  • How big is the market, and is it growing?
  • Who are our main competitors, and what do they do well?
  • What price are people willing to pay?
  • Does our new product idea meet a real need?

Primary vs Secondary Research

FeaturePrimary ResearchSecondary Research
SourceNew data collected directly by the businessExisting data collected by someone else
MethodsSurveys, interviews, focus groups, observation, test marketsGovernment data (ONS), trade reports, competitor websites, news articles
CostHigh — takes time & staffLow — often free or cheap
RelevanceVery high — tailored to the exact questionLower — may not match the exact question
AccuracyVery high — you control qualityLower — depends on original source
Up-to-date?Yes — collected todaySometimes out of date

Quantitative vs Qualitative Research

FeatureQuantitativeQualitative
Type of dataNumerical — percentages, scores, countsText — quotes, stories, observations
MethodsLarge online surveys, sales data, website analyticsInterviews, focus groups, customer observation
StrengthAnswers "how many" — good for statisticsAnswers "why" — good for understanding
WeaknessDoes not explain the reasons behind answersSmall samples — cannot generalise
Best usedMeasuring market size, pricing, satisfaction scoresExploring motivation, uncovering unmet needs

Methods of Market Research (Detail)

MethodWhat It IsBest For
Online SurveyQuestionnaire sent via email, website or app. Quick, cheap, scalable.Measuring satisfaction, preferences, pricing willingness.
Face-to-Face SurveyInterviewer asks questions on the street or in-store.Local market research, rich observational detail.
Focus GroupGroup of 6–10 target customers discuss a product or idea with a moderator.Exploring reactions to new packaging, adverts, concepts.
ObservationWatching customers shop, use a product, or behave in a store.Understanding real behaviour, not claimed behaviour.
Test MarketLaunching a product in a small area before full national launch.Reducing risk of big national launches.
Big Data / AnalyticsAnalysing huge data sets from website clicks, loyalty cards, social media.Segmenting customers, predicting demand, personalising offers.

ICT in Market Research

How Technology Is Reshaping Research
  • Online surveys (SurveyMonkey, Typeform) reach thousands of customers cheaply.
  • Social media listening tracks what people are saying in real time.
  • Website analytics (Google Analytics) show which pages convert, where users drop off.
  • AI can spot patterns in data that a human would miss.
  • Loyalty card data (Clubcard, Nectar) reveals actual buying behaviour, not claimed behaviour.

The Marketing Mix — The 4 Ps

Spec ref: 3.5.4 · Price, Product, Promotion, Place — the most examined area of Paper 2

What Is the Marketing Mix?

The marketing mix is the four elements a business controls to sell its products: Product, Price, Promotion and Place. The best businesses tune all four to reinforce each other — a premium product needs premium pricing, premium promotion and premium distribution.

Price — the Value Signal

Price is what the customer pays. It also signals quality, position and brand. Get price wrong and the best product in the world will not sell.

Pricing StrategyWhat It IsWhen to Use
Cost-PlusAdd a mark-up to the cost of production. Simple, predictable.Stable markets, easy-to-cost products.
Penetration PricingSet a deliberately low price to enter a market and grow share.Launching into a competitive market (e.g. new streaming service).
Price SkimmingSet a high initial price, then reduce it over time.New tech launches — iPhone, PlayStation, innovative gadgets.
Competitive PricingSet the price close to competitors in the same market.Mature markets where price differences are quickly noticed.
Promotional / DiscountTemporarily reduced price to boost short-term sales.Seasonal sales, Black Friday, clearance.
Psychological PricingUse prices like £9.99 that feel much cheaper than £10.00.Supermarkets, retailers, budget brands.
Dynamic PricingPrices change in real-time based on demand (airlines, Uber).Peak-demand services — transport, hotels, events.

Product — the Value Promise

Product covers design, quality, features, branding and packaging. A great product solves a customer need better than alternatives.

The Product Life Cycle
  • Introduction: launched, low sales, high marketing spend, often losses.
  • Growth: sales rise rapidly, profits improve, competitors take notice.
  • Maturity: sales peak, competition intense, profit per unit may fall.
  • Decline: sales fall, product is replaced or discontinued.
Boston Matrix (Quick Summary)
  • Stars: high growth, high share — invest.
  • Cash Cows: low growth, high share — harvest.
  • Problem Children: high growth, low share — decide: invest or divest.
  • Dogs: low growth, low share — usually drop.

Extending the Product Life Cycle

Extension Strategies

  • Re-brand or redesign the product.
  • New packaging or pack sizes.
  • Find new markets (different countries, different ages).
  • Reduce price to extend maturity.
  • Add new features or variants (Kit Kat Chunky, Honey Monster).

When Extension Fails

  • The underlying product is obsolete (Blackberry, Blockbuster).
  • Extensions are cosmetic, not substantive.
  • Costs of extension exceed the extra revenue.
  • Customers now want something fundamentally different.

Promotion — Telling the Story

Promotion is how the business communicates with the customer — building awareness, preference and loyalty.

MethodWhat It IsBest Suited To
AdvertisingPaid messages via TV, radio, print, billboards, online.Mass-market products, brand awareness.
Social MediaContent on Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, Facebook, X.Reaching younger audiences, user-generated campaigns.
SponsorshipPaying to associate the brand with an event, team or person.Building reputation and brand recall (Emirates & football).
Sales PromotionShort-term incentives: BOGOF, coupons, competitions.Driving short-term sales, clearing stock.
Public Relations (PR)Media coverage, press releases, influencer campaigns.Managing reputation, responding to crises.
Direct MarketingEmail, texts, direct mail to specific named customers.Existing customer bases, loyalty-driven sales.
Influencer MarketingSocial media personalities promoting products to their followers.Fashion, beauty, lifestyle, youth markets.
Nishkam Lens — Truthful Promotion

Promotion is where ethics and commerce meet. Is the claim true? Does the advert exploit emotion? Does it target a group who cannot protect themselves? Virtuous promotion builds trust; manipulative promotion erodes it — and eventually destroys the brand.

Place — Where the Customer Finds You

Place is how the product reaches the customer — the distribution strategy. E-commerce has transformed this P more than any other in the last 15 years.

ChannelDescriptionExamples
Physical RetailBricks-and-mortar store selling to walk-in customers.Sainsbury's, Next, John Lewis.
E-commerce (own site)Business sells directly via its own website or app.Apple.com, Nike.com, direct-to-consumer brands.
Online MarketplacesSells via third-party platforms like Amazon, eBay, Etsy.Small businesses selling via Amazon Marketplace.
WholesalersSells in bulk to intermediaries who then sell to retailers.Booker Wholesale (owned by Tesco), Makro.
Direct Mail-OrderSells via catalogue or phone.Boden, JD Williams.
Click-and-CollectOrder online, collect at a store.Argos, Currys, Next.
Impact of E-commerce
  • Removes the need for a high-street presence for many businesses.
  • Huge reach — a tiny start-up can sell globally from day one.
  • Richer data on customer behaviour and preferences.
  • Disrupted traditional retail — BHS, Debenhams, Toys R Us all closed.
  • Created opportunities for direct-to-consumer brands (Gymshark, Glossier).

The Integrated Marketing Mix

Spec ref: 3.5.4 extension — how the 4 Ps reinforce each other

What Does "Integrated" Mean?

The 4 Ps must work together to send one consistent message. A premium product priced cheaply, advertised with amateur graphics and sold in discount shops is a muddled mix — and customers sense it. Great marketing is coherent marketing.

Case Studies — Integration in Action

Premium Integration
Apple

Product: beautifully designed, highly engineered, a closed ecosystem.
Price: premium — no discounting, even at launch.
Promotion: cinematic, elegant, product-focused advertising.
Place: own stores (pristine), own website, selected Premium Resellers.
Every P reinforces "premium". A customer who sees any piece of Apple marketing is reminded of the whole.

Value Integration
Aldi

Product: narrow range, mostly own-label, quality core staples.
Price: lowest in the market (promoted openly).
Promotion: ads that openly compare with Tesco/Sainsbury's on price.
Place: efficient stores, limited decoration, tight local networks.
Every P reinforces "value".

When the Mix Breaks

Examples of Misalignment
  • New Coke (1985): research said customers preferred sweeter taste, but promotion failed to respect brand heritage. Relaunched original within 3 months.
  • JC Penney (2012): dropped discounts in favour of "everyday low prices" but its customer base loved the thrill of sales. Revenue collapsed.
  • Kodak: held on to film at premium prices while the market moved to digital. Product and place were obsolete; the rest of the mix could not save it.

9-Mark Exemplar: Should Freshly Ltd invest in primary market research?

Case Study — Freshly Ltd (practice item)

Freshly Ltd is a healthy meal-kit subscription business with 42,000 customers across the UK. Monthly customer churn has risen from 4% to 7% over the past year. The Marketing Director is considering commissioning a full primary research project — focus groups with 120 customers and an online survey of 8,000 — at a cost of £85,000. The alternative is to rely on the free secondary data the industry association already publishes.

Model 9-Mark Answer

Q: Recommend whether Freshly Ltd should invest £85,000 in primary market research. Give reasons for your recommendation. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR investing (4 chains + data):
An advantage of investing in primary research is that Freshly Ltd would gather data that is specific to its own customers rather than generic industry averages. The research involves focus groups with 120 customers and a survey of 8,000, at a cost of £85,000. This is because primary research is designed for the business's exact research question — in this case, why churn has risen from 4% to 7%. This means that Freshly Ltd could identify whether the cause is pricing, recipe variety, delivery reliability or competitor switching — none of which generic industry reports would reveal. As a result, the Marketing Director could target the real driver of churn with a precise response — for example, changing recipes if variety is the issue or cutting price if pricing is the issue. Therefore, the £85,000 investment could reduce churn back toward the previous 4% rate, saving the business far more in lost subscription revenue than the research itself costs.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view would recognise that £85,000 is a significant sum for a subscription business that is already losing customers at a faster rate. A further limitation is that primary research takes time — focus groups need recruiting, surveys need analysing — meaning Freshly Ltd could lose thousands more customers during the 3–4 month research period. It also depends on whether customers who have already left will honestly answer research questions, or whether only loyal customers respond — skewing the findings. A more balanced view would also note that the secondary data from the industry association is free and available immediately, which could give a first indication of whether this is an industry-wide churn problem or one specific to Freshly Ltd.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe Freshly Ltd should invest in the primary research — but run it in parallel with a rapid review of the free secondary data so no time is wasted. Although £85,000 is a significant upfront cost, this is outweighed by the cost of doing nothing — churn has almost doubled from 4% to 7% and without specific data the Marketing Director is guessing at the cause. However, this depends on whether Freshly Ltd can run a short pilot survey of just the customers who recently cancelled, which could surface the biggest churn driver in weeks rather than months. The most important factor is that subscription businesses live or die on lifetime customer value: retaining an existing subscriber costs roughly one-fifth what it costs to acquire a new one. In the short term the £85,000 will hurt the cash flow, but in the long term the knowledge gained will make every marketing pound more efficient for years. Therefore, Freshly Ltd should invest — starting immediately with a short cancelled-customer survey while the full project is commissioned.

12-Mark Exemplar: Premium pricing or penetration pricing for a new product launch?

Case Study — Lumio Ltd (practice item)
Lumio Ltd

Lumio Ltd is launching a new smart lamp with sleep-tracking technology. The UK smart lamp market is dominated by Philips Hue (market share 48%), with several low-cost Chinese brands (combined market share 22%). Research suggests the Lumio lamp has unique features no competitor currently offers. The Managing Director is choosing between two launch strategies.
Option 1 — Premium pricing: Launch at £149 — 40% above the Philips Hue equivalent. Marketing budget £220,000. Target 8,000 units in year one.
Option 2 — Penetration pricing: Launch at £69 — 30% below Philips Hue. Marketing budget £140,000 (lower because price becomes the marketing message). Target 35,000 units in year one.

Model 12-Mark Answer

Q: Evaluate which of these two pricing strategies will have the biggest impact on Lumio Ltd's market entry and long-term profitability. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Premium pricing at £149 (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of premium pricing is that Lumio Ltd would position the product as a high-status, high-quality item that justifies the unique sleep-tracking feature. At £149 the price is 40% above the Philips Hue equivalent. This is because customers often use price as a signal of quality, especially in technology categories where they cannot easily judge features before purchase. This means that Lumio's unique sleep-tracking feature would be perceived as genuinely valuable rather than being lost among the £69 copycat brands. As a result, the gross profit per unit would be significantly higher — even with lower volumes, the 8,000 target units at £149 generates a strong margin that funds reinvestment in R&D and marketing. Therefore, premium pricing could build a differentiated brand from launch, similar to how Apple established its position. However, this depends on whether customers recognise the sleep-tracking feature as a genuine innovation — or dismiss the lamp as simply overpriced.

Evaluation — Option 1:
A limitation is that the volume target is low — only 8,000 units in year one — so any shortfall in sales is proportionally damaging. It also depends on the Marketing budget of £220,000 being spent effectively on PR and influencer content that educates customers about sleep tracking, rather than on generic advertising.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: Penetration pricing at £69 (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of penetration pricing is that Lumio Ltd would rapidly capture market share in a market dominated by Philips Hue. At £69, the price is 30% below Philips Hue, with a target of 35,000 units. This is because price-sensitive customers who were previously buying the £69 Chinese brands (combined 22% market share) now have access to a feature-rich alternative at the same price. This means that Lumio Ltd could quickly grow brand awareness and recurring customer data — since the lamp connects to an app, every sale builds a user base that can be sold further products or premium subscriptions later. As a result, the 35,000 units in year one gives Lumio Ltd immediate scale advantages, including a stronger negotiating position with retailers and manufacturers. Therefore, penetration pricing could build the business into a genuine challenger to Philips Hue within 2–3 years. However, this depends on whether Lumio Ltd's manufacturing costs are low enough to generate sufficient profit at the £69 price point.

Evaluation — Option 2:
A limitation is that once a brand launches at £69, raising prices later is extremely difficult — customers anchor their expectations to the launch price. It also depends on whether Lumio Ltd can survive the thin margins per unit long enough to convert early customers into higher-value subscribers.

Conclusion — 5 Steps:
To conclude, I think that penetration pricing will have the biggest impact on Lumio Ltd's market entry and long-term profitability. Although premium pricing would generate a stronger perceived brand, this is outweighed by the fact that Philips Hue already holds 48% market share — Lumio Ltd has no existing brand authority to justify a 40% premium, and risks being invisible to customers who default to the category leader. However, this depends on whether Lumio Ltd can secure low enough manufacturing costs to generate at least a small profit at £69 — possibly by partnering with a higher-volume contract manufacturer in year one. The most important factor is that smart lamps are a platform product: the real long-term profit comes from software subscriptions (sleep analysis, coaching, premium features) rather than the hardware itself — so getting 35,000 devices into homes matters more than the profit on each device. In the short term penetration pricing will hurt per-unit margins, but in the long term the large installed base will allow Lumio Ltd to generate recurring software revenue at much higher margins. Therefore, penetration pricing is the stronger strategy because it acquires the customer base on which the long-term business depends.

Exam Technique Masterclass

How to answer every question type for maximum marks
1–2Marks — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase, or single sentence. No explanation needed.
  • Read ALL four options before selecting (MCQs).
  • Use elimination for MCQs.
  • Never write more than one sentence for 1 mark.
2Marks — Explain
Point + development. Two clear sentences. ONE chain of analysis.
  • Rule: marks minus one = number of chains.
  • State the point, then develop using a connective.
4Marks — Explain with context
Point → Explain → Impact. THREE chains of analysis.
  • MUST use case study data where given.
  • Link impact back to the business in the question.
6Marks — Analyse
Point → Evidence → Explain with THREE chains → Link to outcome
  • Use case study evidence in blue.
  • End with a clear outcome for the business.
From 2026, "analyse one reason" now requires TWO developed reasons/points.
9Marks — Recommend
THREE paragraphs: (1) Argument with 4 chains + data manipulation, (2) Purely evaluative — "more balanced view / further limitation / depends on", (3) Conclusion: "Overall, I believe" + NEW information + short/long term + "Therefore".
  • Para 1: Point → Evidence from case study → Data manipulation → Consequence → Therefore. Minimum 4 chain connectors.
  • Para 2: Purely evaluative — weigh risks and limitations.
  • Para 3: Clear recommendation with NEW information not in paragraphs 1 or 2. Must include short-term vs long-term reasoning. No fence-sitting.
  • Conclusion that only repeats paragraph 1 = Level 2 maximum.
12Marks — Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 3: Conclusion with 5 steps.
  • BOTH options: 4 chains each. Manipulate data (calculate savings, percentages, compare figures).
  • Each option followed by a short concise evaluation (2–3 sentences).
  • Conclusion 5 steps: (1) "To conclude…" (2) "Although… this is outweighed by…" (3) "However, this depends on…" (4) "The most important factor" + NEW information (5) "Therefore…"
  • NEW information MUST appear in the conclusion — a factor, calculation or stakeholder point not raised in paragraphs 1 or 2.
  • Integrate functional areas — link marketing → operations → finance → HR. Isolated analysis = Level 3 maximum.

Key Terms Glossary — Unit 5

Every definition you need to know for the exam
Marketing
The business function that identifies customers, anticipates their needs and profitably satisfies them.
Market
A place (physical or online) where buyers and sellers come together to trade.
Market Share
A business's sales as a percentage of total sales in the market.
Target Market
The specific group of customers that a business aims its product or service at.
Customer Needs
The essential things a customer must have — food, shelter, transport, safety.
Customer Wants
The things customers would like to have but could live without — luxury cars, designer clothing.
Market Segmentation
Dividing a market into smaller groups of customers with similar needs or characteristics.
Demographic Segmentation
Splitting a market by age, gender, income, occupation or family status.
Geographic Segmentation
Splitting a market by region, country, city or climate.
Behavioural Segmentation
Splitting a market by how customers behave — loyalty, usage rate, purchase occasion.
Psychographic Segmentation
Splitting a market by lifestyle, values, attitudes and interests.
Market Research
The process of gathering and analysing information about customers, competitors and markets.
Primary Research
Original research carried out by the business for its own specific purpose (e.g. survey, focus group).
Secondary Research
Research that uses existing data already collected by someone else (e.g. government statistics, competitor reports).
Qualitative Data
Descriptive information based on opinions, feelings and reasons — typically from interviews or focus groups.
Quantitative Data
Numerical information that can be measured and analysed statistically — typically from surveys.
Sample
A smaller group selected from the target population to represent the whole.
Marketing Mix
The four Ps: Product, Price, Place and Promotion — the controllable elements of marketing strategy.
Product
The good or service a business offers to satisfy customer needs and wants.
Product Life Cycle
The four stages a product passes through: introduction, growth, maturity, decline.
Brand
A name, logo or identity that distinguishes a product and builds customer loyalty.
USP (Unique Selling Point)
The feature that makes a product different from, and better than, its competitors.
Price Skimming
Launching a product at a high price then lowering it over time.
Penetration Pricing
Launching a product at a low price to quickly gain market share.
Competitive Pricing
Setting a price close to the price of competitors to stay in the market.
Cost-Plus Pricing
Setting a price by adding a fixed percentage profit margin to the cost of production.
Loss Leader
Selling a product below cost to attract customers who then buy other profitable items.
Promotion
All the ways a business communicates with customers to inform and persuade them.
Advertising
Paid, non-personal communication — TV, print, online, outdoor.
Sales Promotion
Short-term incentives such as discounts, BOGOF, vouchers or loyalty points.
Public Relations (PR)
Managing how a business is perceived by the public through earned media coverage.
Place
How and where a product reaches the customer — the distribution channel.
Distribution Channel
The route a product takes from producer to final consumer (direct, via wholesaler, via retailer).
Integrated Marketing Mix
When all four Ps work together consistently to deliver one clear message to the target customer.

Grade 9 Revision Checklist

Tick off each item as you master it

3.5.1 Identifying Customers

  • I can define customer needs and customer wants with clear examples.
  • I can explain why identifying the target customer comes before every other marketing decision.
  • I can explain the difference between B2B and B2C customers.
  • I can link identifying customers to a business's survival and growth.

3.5.2 Market Segmentation

  • I can define and give examples of demographic, geographic, behavioural and psychographic segmentation.
  • I can explain the advantages and disadvantages of segmentation to a business.
  • I can evaluate when segmentation is essential and when mass marketing is appropriate.
  • I can apply segmentation to any product or service in a case study.

3.5.3 Market Research

  • I can define and distinguish primary vs secondary research with examples.
  • I can define qualitative vs quantitative data and identify which research methods produce which.
  • I can explain advantages and disadvantages of each research method (survey, focus group, observation, interview).
  • I can recommend a research method for a specific business problem with justification.

3.5.4 The Marketing Mix (4 Ps)

  • I can define each of the 4 Ps: Product, Price, Place, Promotion.
  • I can explain the product life cycle and the marketing actions at each stage.
  • I can explain and compare pricing strategies (skimming, penetration, competitive, cost-plus, loss leader).
  • I can explain promotional methods (advertising, sales promotion, PR, digital) and when each is appropriate.
  • I can explain distribution channels (direct, retailer, wholesaler, online).

3.5.5 The Integrated Marketing Mix

  • I can explain why the 4 Ps must be aligned and reinforce one consistent message.
  • I can apply the integrated mix to evaluate a whole marketing strategy.
  • I can identify inconsistencies between elements of a business's marketing mix in case studies.
