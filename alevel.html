A Level
Business.

What comes next? The new AQA specification, how your GCSE prepares you, how Business compares to Economics, and the university and career pathways it opens up.

New

A brand new specification.

The AQA A Level Business specification is changing. Here is everything you need to know about what is coming and how your GCSE prepares you for it.

New specification: AQA A Level Business (7138)
First teaching September 2026. First AS exams 2027. First A Level exams 2028. The current specification (7132) has its final exams in summer 2026. If you are starting Year 12 in September 2026, you will be on the new spec. It has been completely redesigned with modern, future-focused content including digital technology, ESG reporting, employee wellbeing and business ethics at its core.

AQA describes the new specification as being reimagined for the modern world. The old 10-topic structure has been replaced with a cleaner three-unit model that integrates topics more naturally. The assessment has also changed: simplified command words, consistent paper structures across all three papers, and more time per mark. Real-world case studies are used throughout, and synoptic thinking (connecting ideas across the whole course) is built into Paper 3 from the start.

Structure

Three units, three papers.

The new specification is built around three integrated units. Units 1 and 2 are taught across both years. Unit 3 is A Level only and is fully synoptic.

Bridge

From GCSE to A Level.

Your GCSE Business Studies course gives you a strong foundation. Here is what stays the same, what goes deeper, and what is entirely new.

Builds on GCSE
Marketing
You already know the 4Ps. A Level adds elasticity of demand (price and income), digital marketing strategy (SEO, PPC, CRM, data analytics), correlation analysis, market mapping, and international marketing. You learn to calculate and interpret market data quantitatively.
Builds on GCSE
Finance
GCSE covers cash flow and basic profit. A Level adds income statements, statements of financial position (balance sheets), ROCE, gearing, dividend yield, market capitalisation, return on investment, payables and receivables days, current and acid test ratios, investment appraisal (payback, ARR, NPV), budgeting with variance analysis, and zero-based budgeting. You will also learn to amend financial statements, not just interpret them.
Builds on GCSE
Human Resources
GCSE covers motivation theories and recruitment. A Level goes deeper into employee wellbeing, flexible working, business culture, equality/diversity/inclusion/belonging, and HR planning as a strategic function linked to business objectives.
Entirely new
Strategy and Change
This does not exist at GCSE. A Level introduces strategic models (Ansoff Matrix, Porter's Five Forces, stakeholder mapping), SWOT analysis as a decision-making tool, investment appraisal (payback, ARR, NPV), Carroll's CSR pyramid, ESG reporting, Triple Bottom Line, network analysis with critical path, Lewin's Force Field analysis for managing change, Kotter and Schlesinger's reasons for resistance, scenario planning, and the distinction between risk and uncertainty.
Determination in action
Moving from GCSE to A Level Business requires a step change in the depth and sophistication of your thinking. The content is broader, the analysis must be deeper, and the evaluation must be sharper. But the chains-of-analysis skills you have built at GCSE are exactly the foundation you need. Every 9-mark and 12-mark answer you have practised has been training you for this.
Compare

A Level Business vs A Level Economics.

Both are excellent subjects, but they are fundamentally different. Understanding the distinction will help you make the right choice for your interests and ambitions.

A Level Business studies how individual organisations operate. You analyse real companies, make decisions about marketing, finance, HR and operations, and evaluate strategic options using case studies. It is practical, applied, and rooted in how businesses actually work.

A Level Economics studies how entire economies function. You learn about supply and demand, market structures, inflation, unemployment, fiscal and monetary policy, and international trade. It is more theoretical and abstract, with a heavier emphasis on diagrams, models and essay writing.

They are not the same subject, and they are not interchangeable. Some universities (including Oxford, Cambridge and LSE) will count them as too similar if you take both, meaning they would treat your three A Levels as effectively two. If you are considering both, check the entry requirements of the universities you are interested in.

DimensionA Level BusinessA Level Economics
FocusHow individual businesses operate, compete and growHow markets and entire economies function
ApproachApplied and practical — case studies, real companies, decision-makingTheoretical and analytical — models, diagrams, policy evaluation
MathsQuantitative skills (ratios, percentages, break-even, elasticity) but within business contextsMore mathematical — diagrams with axes, elasticity calculations, multiplier effects. An Economics degree usually requires A Level Maths
Writing styleStructured responses using case study evidence, chains of analysis, and supported conclusionsExtended essays requiring theoretical frameworks, diagram annotation, and policy evaluation
Content overlapSome macro topics (interest rates, exchange rates, globalisation) appear in bothSome micro topics (costs, revenue, market structures) appear in both
University perceptionRespected and valued for Business, Management, Accounting, Marketing degrees. Some top universities view it as less academic than EconomicsConsidered a traditional academic subject. Required or strongly preferred for Economics degrees at Russell Group universities
Best forStudents interested in running or managing businesses, marketing, HR, finance, entrepreneurship, or management consultingStudents interested in economic policy, government, banking, financial analysis, research, or academic economics
Key differenceYou study one business at a time and make decisions about itYou study the whole economy and evaluate policies that affect millions of people

The honest advice: If you enjoyed GCSE Business Studies and want to go deeper into how companies actually work, A Level Business is the natural next step. If you found the economic climate section of Unit 2 (interest rates, exchange rates, inflation) the most interesting part of the course and want to explore that world in much more depth, Economics might be for you. If you are aiming for a top Economics degree, you will almost certainly need A Level Maths alongside it.

Can you do both? Yes, but be cautious. Some Russell Group universities will only count one of them towards your entry requirements. Check before you commit. If you want to keep both options open, pairing one of them with A Level Maths is the strongest combination.

Skills

What transfers from GCSE.

The skills you have built in this course are not just for passing an exam. They are the foundation for A Level success and beyond.

Exam technique
Chains of analysis
The chain-building methodology you have practised (point, evidence, consequence, business impact) is identical at A Level. The chains are longer and the context is more complex, but the skill is the same. You are already ahead of students who have not studied Business at GCSE.
Exam technique
Data manipulation
Using case study data to calculate percentages, profit margins, and break-even is a core skill at A Level. You have been doing this since Year 10. At A Level, the calculations are more varied (ROCE, gearing, dividend yield, NPV) but the principle is the same: extract the numbers, calculate, then analyse what they mean.
Critical thinking
Evaluation and judgement
Your 12-mark conclusion framework (To conclude... Although... However, this depends on... The most important factor... Therefore...) is directly transferable. A Level evaluation requires the same structure but with more sophistication and theoretical backing.
Knowledge
Business fundamentals
Revenue, costs, profit, cash flow, stakeholders, marketing mix, motivation theories, organisational structures, production methods, quality management, ethics, globalisation. You already know all of this. A Level builds on it rather than starting from scratch.
Futures

Where A Level Business leads.

University degrees, apprenticeships, and careers that A Level Business opens up.

University degrees: Business Management, Accounting and Finance, Marketing, International Business, Entrepreneurship, Human Resource Management, Supply Chain Management, Business Analytics, Real Estate, Hospitality Management. At Russell Group universities, typical offers range from ABB to AAA depending on the course and institution.

Russell Group universities offering Business/Management degrees: University of Bath, University of Birmingham, University of Bristol, Durham University, University of Edinburgh, University of Exeter, King's College London, Lancaster University, University of Leeds, University of Liverpool, London School of Economics (Management only), University of Manchester, Newcastle University, University of Nottingham, Queen Mary University of London, University of Sheffield, University of Southampton, University of Warwick, University of York.

Degree apprenticeships: Chartered Manager Degree Apprenticeship (Level 6), Digital and Technology Solutions (Level 6), Accountancy/Taxation Professional (Level 7), Senior Leader Apprenticeship (Level 7). These combine full-time paid employment with a degree qualification. Companies offering them include PwC, Deloitte, KPMG, Unilever, Rolls-Royce, BAE Systems, NHS, Civil Service, and many more. No tuition fees. You earn while you learn.

Career paths: Management consulting, financial analysis, investment banking, marketing management, HR management, operations management, supply chain logistics, data analytics, product management, entrepreneurship, public sector management, project management, business journalism.

Peacefulness, Diligence, Determination
Whatever path you choose after GCSE, the three virtues you have practised every lesson will serve you. Peacefulness gives you the composure to think clearly under pressure. Diligence gives you the discipline to master complex material. Determination gives you the resilience to push through when the work gets hard. These are not just classroom behaviours. They are the qualities that employers, universities and communities value most.
