The Purpose of Business Activity

Spec ref: 3.1.1 · Tested every year across Paper 1 & Paper 2

Why Do Businesses Exist?

A business exists to provide goods (physical products) or services (non-physical things) to meet the needs and wants of customers.

Key Definitions
  • Need: Something essential for survival — food, water, shelter, clothing, warmth.
  • Want: Something desired but not essential — designer clothes, holidays, latest smartphone.
  • Good: A physical, tangible product that can be touched (e.g. a pair of boots, a phone case).
  • Service: An intangible activity performed for a customer (e.g. hairdressing, web design, banking).

Sectors of Production

SectorWhat It DoesExamples

SecondaryManufactures/processes raw materials into finished goods.Car manufacturing, construction, food processing, clothing.
TertiaryProvides services to businesses and consumers.Banking, retail, hairdressing, education, transport.

Key Definitions
  • Enterprise: The willingness to take risks and show initiative in starting and running a business — one of the four factors of production.
  • Entrepreneur: A person who takes the financial risk of starting and running a new business venture, organising the other factors of production (land, labour, capital).

Sustainability & Business

Key Definition

Sustainability: Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. A sustainable business considers its long-term environmental and social impact, not just short-term profit.

Businesses are under growing pressure from consumers, governments and investors to operate sustainably. This includes reducing waste, lowering carbon emissions, sourcing materials ethically, and considering the environmental impact of all business decisions.

Benefits of Operating Sustainably

  • Attracts environmentally conscious customers — growing market segment.
  • Improves brand image and reputation — differentiates from less ethical competitors.
  • Reduces long-term costs through energy efficiency and waste reduction.
  • Helps attract and retain employees who want to work for responsible businesses.

Challenges of Sustainability

  • Higher initial costs — sustainable materials, renewable energy and ethical suppliers are often more expensive.
  • May reduce short-term profit, creating conflict with shareholders who prioritise dividends.
  • Difficult to measure — environmental impact is harder to quantify than financial performance.
  • Risk of greenwashing — businesses that claim to be sustainable without genuine action face reputational damage.

Opportunity Cost

Key DefinitionOpportunity Cost = the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made

Why Businesses Change Ownership Structure

As businesses grow, they often change their legal structure to access more capital, reduce personal risk or gain credibility. A typical progression might be:

Common Progression
  • Sole trader → Partnership: To share the workload, bring in new skills, or raise additional capital from a partner.
  • Partnership → Private Ltd (Ltd): To gain limited liability, protecting personal assets as the business takes on more financial risk.
  • Private Ltd (Ltd) → Public Ltd (Plc): To raise very large amounts of capital by selling shares on the stock exchange — often needed for national or international expansion.
Key Point — Not-for-Profit vs For-Profit
  • For-profit businesses (sole traders, partnerships, Ltd, Plc) exist primarily to generate profit for their owners or shareholders.
  • Not-for-profit organisations (social enterprises, charities, community interest companies) trade to generate revenue, but reinvest all surplus into their social or environmental mission rather than distributing it to owners.
  • A social enterprise operates commercially — it sells goods/services like any other business — but its primary purpose is social benefit, not private enrichment.

Setting Business Aims & Objectives

Spec ref: 3.1.3 · Tested frequently — especially objectives changing over time

Aims vs Objectives

Key Definitions
  • Aim: A broad, long-term goal that gives the business overall direction (e.g. "to be the market leader in sustainable fashion").
  • Objective: A specific, measurable target set to help achieve the aim (e.g. "increase sales by 10% this year" or "reduce waste by 25% within three years").
Business ObjectiveWhat It MeansTypical Example
SurvivalStaying in business — especially critical in the first 1–2 years or during a recession. Many new businesses fail within their first year.A new restaurant focuses on covering its costs month-to-month rather than maximising profit.
ProfitEarning more revenue than total costs. Profit can be reinvested, saved or distributed to owners.A sole trader aims to make £40,000 profit this year — enough to live on and grow.
GrowthIncreasing the size of the business — measured by sales revenue, number of employees, output or number of locations.A bakery opens a second store in a neighbouring town.
Market shareIncreasing the percentage of total market sales that the business controls, relative to its competitors.A phone brand aims to increase its UK market share from 12% to 15%.
Customer satisfactionEnsuring customers are happy with the quality of products, services and overall experience.A hotel targets a 4.5-star average rating on review platforms.
Social / ethical objectivesOperating in a way that benefits society — beyond what the law requires. Includes fair trade, environmental responsibility and community support.A coffee company commits to paying farmers above market price for ethically sourced beans.

Why Objectives Change Over Time

A business does not keep the same objectives forever. As the business grows, as the market changes, or as external pressures shift, objectives must adapt.

Stage / SituationLikely ObjectiveWhy It Changes
New / start-up businessSurvivalThe business has limited customers, high set-up costs and no track record — staying open is the priority.
Established businessProfit and growthOnce survival is secured, the focus shifts to increasing profit, expanding and building market share.
During a recessionSurvival (again)Consumer spending falls, costs may rise — even large businesses may refocus on simply staying afloat.
Facing competitionMarket share / customer satisfactionNew rivals entering the market force the business to focus on keeping existing customers and winning new ones.
Growing public pressureSocial / ethical / environmentalConsumer expectations, media scrutiny and legislation push businesses towards more sustainable and ethical practices.
Key Point — Objectives Often Conflict
  • A business that prioritises profit maximisation may cut employee wages — conflicting with employee satisfaction.
  • A business that pursues rapid growth may compromise customer satisfaction if quality drops.
  • Pursuing ethical objectives (e.g. Fairtrade sourcing) may increase costs and reduce short-term profit.

Stakeholders

Spec ref: 3.1.4 · Key topic for stakeholder conflict questions

Who Are Stakeholders?

DefinitionA stakeholder is any individual or group that has an interest in, or is affected by, the activities of a business.
StakeholderWhat They Want
Owners / ShareholdersProfit, dividends, growth, return on investment.
EmployeesFair pay, job security, good working conditions, training.
CustomersGood quality products/services, fair prices, good customer service.
SuppliersRegular orders, prompt payment, long-term contracts.
Local CommunityJobs, minimal pollution/noise, support for local projects.
GovernmentTax revenue, job creation, compliance with laws.

Stakeholder Influence & Importance

Not all stakeholders have equal power. Some can directly affect business decisions (e.g. shareholders can vote to remove directors), while others have indirect influence (e.g. the local community can campaign against a new development). Understanding who has the most influence and why is essential for longer-answer questions.

StakeholderHow They Influence the Business
ShareholdersVote on major decisions at AGMs, can sell shares (reducing share price), can appoint or remove directors. The more shares held, the more power they have.
EmployeesCan take industrial action (strikes, work-to-rule), leave for competitors (increasing recruitment costs), or reduce productivity if demotivated.
CustomersCan stop buying products, leave negative reviews, switch to competitors, or campaign on social media — directly affecting revenue.
SuppliersCan increase prices, reduce credit terms, prioritise other customers, or refuse to supply — disrupting the business's operations.
Local communityCan support or oppose planning applications, organise protests, generate positive or negative media coverage.
GovernmentCan change laws, increase taxes, introduce regulations, offer grants or subsidies — directly shaping the business environment.

Stakeholder Conflicts

Different stakeholders often want different things from the same business. When one group's interests are prioritised, another group may lose out. This is known as stakeholder conflict.

ConflictStakeholder A Wants…Stakeholder B Wants…Why They Conflict
Shareholders vs EmployeesHigher profits / dividendsHigher wages / better conditionsHigher wages increase costs and reduce profit available for dividends.
Shareholders vs Local CommunityCost reduction / expansionMinimal pollution / noise / disruptionExpansion may damage the local environment or increase traffic, harming residents.
Customers vs ShareholdersLower prices / higher qualityHigher profit marginsCutting prices or improving quality increases costs and reduces profit per unit.
Employees vs CustomersShorter working hoursLonger opening hours / faster serviceMeeting customer expectations for convenience may require employees to work unsociable hours.
Suppliers vs Business (as customer)Higher prices / prompt paymentLowest possible costs / extended creditThe business wants to minimise costs; the supplier wants to maximise their own revenue.

Model Answer — 2 marks

Shareholders wanting higher profits may conflict with employees who want higher wages. This is because increasing wages would increase the business's costs, reducing the amount of profit available to distribute as dividends to shareholders.

Stakeholder 1: Employees — they have a direct interest in their wages, working conditions and job security.

Stakeholder 2: Suppliers — they are affected by the business's purchasing decisions, payment terms and order volumes. Any two from: shareholders, customers, employees, local community, suppliers, competitors, government, lenders.

Business Location

Spec ref: 3.1.5 · Often tested via case studies

Factors Affecting Location

The location of a business can be the difference between success and failure. Different types of business prioritise different factors — a factory has very different location needs to a coffee shop.

FactorWhy It MattersMost Important For…
Proximity to customersBeing close to customers increases footfall and convenience. Retail businesses depend heavily on passing trade and easy access.Retail shops, restaurants, cafés, hairdressers.
Proximity to materials / suppliersReduces transport costs and delivery times. Particularly important for businesses that use heavy, bulky or perishable raw materials.Factories, food processors, construction firms.
Proximity to labourAccess to a skilled or affordable workforce. Businesses need to locate where suitable employees are available and willing to work.Technology firms (near universities), manufacturing.
Costs (rent and wages)Rent in city centres is far higher than in rural or suburban areas. Wage expectations also vary by region. Lower costs mean higher profit margins.Start-ups and small businesses with limited budgets.
Infrastructure & transport linksGood road, rail, port or airport connections make it easier to receive supplies and distribute products to customers across the country or internationally.Warehouses, distribution centres, exporters.
CompetitionSome businesses benefit from being near competitors (e.g. restaurant districts attract more customers overall). Others avoid competitors to reduce direct rivalry.Retailers in shopping centres vs sole traders avoiding high streets.
The internet / e-commerceOnline businesses can sell to customers anywhere. E-commerce reduces the importance of physical location — a website can reach millions from a small warehouse.Online retailers, digital service providers, freelancers.

The Impact of Technology on Location

Technology — and e-commerce in particular — has fundamentally changed how businesses think about location. The growth of the internet means many businesses no longer need a physical high street presence to reach customers.

How Technology Reduces Location Importance

  • E-commerce websites allow businesses to sell 24/7 to customers across the country or worldwide — no high street needed.
  • Social media marketing reaches targeted audiences regardless of where the business is physically based.
  • Cloud computing and video conferencing allow employees to work remotely, reducing the need for expensive city-centre offices.
  • Businesses can operate from cheaper locations (e.g. industrial estates, home offices) and pass savings on to customers through lower prices.

Why Location Still Matters

  • Some services must be delivered in person — hairdressers, restaurants, dental surgeries, gyms.
  • Customers may want to see, touch or try on physical products before buying — especially for expensive items like furniture or clothing.
  • Delivery costs can be significant for online businesses, especially for heavy or bulky items.
  • Physical stores build trust and brand presence in ways that websites alone cannot always replicate.

What Is a Business Plan?

Key Definition

Business plan: A written document that sets out what a business intends to achieve and how it plans to achieve it. It is used to guide decision-making and to persuade lenders or investors to provide finance.

Section of a Business PlanWhat It Contains
Business ideaA clear description of the product or service, and what makes it different from competitors.
Business aims & objectivesWhat the business wants to achieve in the short term and long term (e.g. survival, profit targets, growth goals).
Target marketWho the business will sell to — customer demographics, location, needs and spending habits.
Revenue forecastA prediction of expected sales revenue based on market research, pricing and estimated demand.
Costs forecastAn estimate of all fixed and variable costs the business expects to incur.
Cash flow forecastA month-by-month prediction of money flowing in and out of the business — to check whether the business can pay its bills on time.
Sources of financeHow the business will fund its start-up and running costs (personal savings, bank loans, investors, etc.).
Marketing planHow the business will attract and retain customers (pricing, promotion, distribution).

Advantages of a Business Plan

  • Helps the entrepreneur think through every aspect of the business before committing money.
  • Essential for securing loans or investment — banks and investors will not lend without one.
  • Provides clear targets to measure actual performance against.
  • Identifies potential risks and challenges early, so the owner can plan how to deal with them.

Limitations of a Business Plan

  • Based on forecasts and estimates — actual results may differ significantly from predictions.
  • The business environment is dynamic — unexpected changes (economic downturn, new competitor) can make the plan outdated quickly.
  • Time-consuming to prepare — time spent planning is time not spent running the business.
  • Can create a false sense of security — having a plan does not guarantee success.

Fixed Costs vs Variable Costs

Fixed Costs

  • Costs that stay the same regardless of how much the business produces or sells.
  • Examples: Rent, insurance, salaries of permanent staff, loan repayments, business rates.
  • A shop pays the same rent whether it sells 10 items or 10,000 items in a month.

Variable Costs

  • Costs that change with the level of output — the more you produce, the higher the variable costs.
  • Examples: Raw materials, packaging, delivery costs, production-line wages (per unit).
  • A bakery that makes 200 loaves uses twice as much flour as one that makes 100 loaves.
RememberTotal Costs = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs

To calculate total variable costs: Variable cost per unit × Number of units produced.

Expanding a Business

Spec ref: 3.1.7 · Very high frequency — economies/diseconomies, organic vs external growth

Methods of Growth

Organic (Internal) Growth

  • Expanding using own resources — opening new stores, launching new products, hiring staff, e-commerce.
  • Slower but lower risk.
  • Business retains full control.

External Growth

  • Merger: Two businesses agree to join together.
  • Takeover: One business buys another (may be hostile).
  • Faster growth but higher risk — culture clashes, integration problems.

Types of Integration (External Growth)

When businesses grow externally through mergers or takeovers, the type of integration depends on the relationship between the two businesses involved.

TypeWhat It MeansExampleKey Benefit
Horizontal integrationTwo businesses at the same stage of production and in the same industry merge or one takes over the other.Two car manufacturers merge; one supermarket chain buys another.Increased market share, reduced competition, economies of scale.
Forward vertical integrationA business merges with or takes over a business at a later stage in the supply chain (closer to the customer).A farmer buys a chain of farm shops to sell directly to consumers.Greater control over how products are sold; higher profit by cutting out the middleman.
Backward vertical integrationA business merges with or takes over a business at an earlier stage in the supply chain (closer to raw materials).A car manufacturer buys a steel producer.Secures supply of raw materials; reduces costs and dependence on suppliers.
Conglomerate integrationTwo businesses in completely different industries merge — they have no direct connection in production or supply.A technology company buys a food brand.Diversification — reduces risk because the business is not dependent on one industry.
Common Confusion
  • A merger is when two businesses agree to join together as equals.
  • A takeover is when one business buys another — this may be agreed (friendly) or against the wishes of the target company (hostile).

Economies of Scale

Key ConceptAs output increases, average unit cost FALLS. AQA specifically tests purchasing and technical economies.
TypeExplanation
PurchasingLarger orders = bulk buying discounts from suppliers → lower cost per unit.
TechnicalLarger businesses can afford specialist machinery that produces goods more cheaply.
FinancialLarger businesses borrow at lower interest rates (seen as lower risk).
ManagerialCan employ specialist managers — expertise spread over more output.

Diseconomies of Scale

When a business grows TOO large, average unit costs may start to rise due to: poor communication, coordination issues, and reduced staff motivation.

  • C Lower average unit costs
  • D Poor communication
Why D is correctPoor communication is a cause of diseconomies of scale — messages get distorted across many layers.

Franchising

Key Definitions
  • Franchise: A legal agreement where one business (the franchisor) grants another party (the franchisee) the right to trade using its brand, products and business model in exchange for fees and royalties.
  • Franchisor: The business that owns the brand and sells the right to use it (e.g. McDonald's, Subway, Domino's).
  • Franchisee: The person or business that pays to operate under the franchisor's established brand and systems.

Advantages for the Franchisor

  • Rapid expansion without the full cost of opening new locations — the franchisee provides the capital.
  • Earns ongoing royalty income from every franchisee.
  • Lower risk — the franchisee takes on the day-to-day financial risk of running each outlet.

Advantages for the Franchisee

  • Trades under an established, trusted brand — customers already know and trust the name.
  • Receives training, marketing support and a proven business model — reducing the risk of failure.
  • Easier to secure bank loans — lenders view franchises as lower risk than independent start-ups.
Disadvantages of Franchising
  • For the franchisor: A poorly run franchise can damage the entire brand's reputation. Less direct control over quality and customer experience.
  • For the franchisee: Must pay ongoing royalties and fees which reduce profit. Limited freedom — must follow the franchisor's rules on products, pricing, décor and operations.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view recognises significant risks that the financial case alone does not capture. Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe Premium Motors should NOT take over LLC. Although the immediate hotel contracts are attractive, this is outweighed by the fact that the contracts expire within 12 months and Janet has no London market knowledge to win replacements. The most important factor is that the £400,000 is a sunk cost if the London expansion fails — and the relocation cost alone represents a significant proportion of a small business's reserves that cannot be recovered. In the short term the takeover looks attractive for speed of entry, but in the long term organic growth in Manchester carries far lower risk and protects Premium Motors' established reputation. 9-Mark Exemplar: Should John form a partnership with Lucas? (2020 P1 Q2.4)

Case Study — Item A: Adventures (2020 P1)

John is a sole trader who owns Adventures, selling activity holidays. He wants to expand into ski holidays in Italy. Lucas has lived and worked in an Italian ski resort for several years. He is familiar with the local hotels and knows several ski instructors. Lucas does not believe that the business should pay a premium price for hotels but is keen to invest. Lucas wants decisions shared equally with John.

Model 9-Mark Answer

Q: Recommend whether John should form a partnership with Lucas. Give reasons for your recommendation. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR partnership (4 chains + data):
An advantage of forming a partnership with Lucas is that he brings valuable local knowledge. Lucas has lived and worked in the Italian ski resort of Folgaria for several years and is familiar with local hotels and ski instructors. This is because this local expertise would allow Adventures to set up ski holidays quickly without John having to research hotels, transport and instructors from scratch — a process that could take months and cost significant money if done independently. This means that Adventures could offer high-quality ski packages with trusted hotels and instructors from the first season, generating revenue immediately rather than spending John's aim of doubling profits within the first year on research alone. As a result, Lucas's contacts could help Adventures attract customers faster than any competitor entering the Italian ski market without local connections. Therefore the partnership could have the biggest impact on expanding Adventures successfully into a new market.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view would consider the significant risks of this partnership. Lucas does not believe the business should pay a premium price for hotels, but John strongly believes a successful business should be sustainable. This suggests that there could be serious and repeated disagreements about quality, ethics and supplier relationships that could damage Adventures' existing reputation — a brand John has spent seven years building. A further limitation is that Lucas wants decisions shared equally, yet Lucas has never run his own business before — giving equal decision-making power to someone with no business management experience is a significant risk. It also depends on whether Lucas would accept a compromise on hotel quality, or whether this fundamental disagreement would lead to conflict that paralyses the business entirely.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe John should NOT form a partnership with Lucas. Although Lucas's local knowledge would accelerate expansion into Italy, this is outweighed by the fundamental disagreement over sustainability and hotel quality — which is not a minor operational detail but a core brand value. However, this depends on whether John could hire Lucas as a paid consultant instead — gaining access to his Italian contacts and local expertise without giving up 50% of decision-making power or ownership. The most important factor is that partnerships with unlimited liability mean John's personal assets are at risk if Lucas's cost-cutting approach damages the brand and the business fails. In the short term the partnership looks attractive for speed of entry, but in the long term protecting the sustainable brand John has built over seven years is far more valuable than one season of faster expansion. Therefore, John should explore a consultancy arrangement with Lucas rather than a full partnership.

12-Mark Exemplar: CP — Convert to Plc or Reduce Cost of Supplies? (2025 P1 Q3.8)

Case Study — Item E: Charlie Paul (2025 P1)
Charlie Paul (CP) — Family Retailer

CP is a family-owned business which prides itself on quality products and services. Prices are high and rarely reduced. CP has an outstanding reputation for treating employees and suppliers well. The business has never made employees redundant in 50 years. Suppliers are paid after 35 days at fair prices to support Fairtrade producers. Increasing competition from online retailers is reducing CP's sales. A large amount of cash is needed to develop its website.
Option 1 — Convert to a Plc: Sell 60% of shares to the public. Naia Hayat, a well-known business woman, may invest. She managed successful retail outlets by keeping staff costs to a minimum to maximise profits.
Option 2 — Reducing cost of supplies: A large manufacturer offers products at slightly lower quality but half the current price if CP buys 65% of stock from them.

Model 12-Mark Answer

Q: Evaluate which of these two options will have the biggest impact on CP's ability to maintain its current objectives. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Converting to a Plc (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of converting to a Plc is that CP would be able to raise a large amount of cash by selling 60% of its shares to the general public. This is because being listed on the stock exchange gives access to millions of potential investors, generating the significant funds needed to develop CP's website to compete with the online retailers that are reducing CP's sales. This means that CP could invest heavily in e-commerce and digital marketing, potentially reversing the decline in sales and reaching customers who currently cannot access CP's stores — expanding from a regional to a national customer base. As a result, CP could significantly increase revenue, which would offset the competitive pressure from online rivals and protect long-term profitability. Therefore converting to a Plc could generate the capital needed for the digital transformation that CP urgently requires. However, this depends on whether the new shareholders — particularly Naia Hayat, who managed successful retail outlets by keeping staff costs to a minimum to maximise profits — would respect CP's current objectives or push for cost-cutting that contradicts the company's values.

Evaluation — Option 1:
A more balanced assessment is that selling 60% of shares means the family loses majority control — external shareholders could outvote them on every major decision. This is significant because Naia Hayat's approach of minimising staff costs directly contradicts CP's 50-year commitment to never making employees redundant, risking the loss of experienced staff and the premium reputation that justifies CP's high prices.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: Reducing the cost of supplies (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of reducing supply costs is that CP could dramatically improve profit margins without needing external investors. The new manufacturer offers products at half the current price if CP buys 65% of stock from them. This is because bulk buying from a single large manufacturer is far cheaper than sourcing from multiple small Fairtrade producers. This means that CP's cost of goods sold could fall by up to 50% on 65% of its product range — a saving that could generate substantial funds for website development while keeping every penny of ownership within the family. As a result, CP would have the cash to invest in e-commerce and compete with online retailers without sacrificing any ownership, control or decision-making power. Therefore this option addresses the immediate competitive threat while fully protecting CP's independence.

Evaluation — Option 2:
However, a limitation is that the slightly lower quality products could damage CP's premium brand if customers notice the difference. It also depends on whether relying on one manufacturer for 65% of stock creates a dangerous dependency — if that supplier fails or raises prices, CP has no alternative. By contrast, Option 1 raises capital without changing product quality at all.

Conclusion — 5 Steps:
To conclude, I think that Option 2 (reducing supply costs) will have the biggest impact on CP's ability to maintain its current objectives. Although converting to a Plc would raise more capital and enable faster digital transformation, this is outweighed by the fact that selling 60% of shares means the family permanently loses majority control — a decision that cannot be reversed — and Naia Hayat's cost-cutting philosophy is fundamentally incompatible with CP's values. However, this depends on whether CP negotiates with the new manufacturer to maintain quality standards as a condition of the contract, ensuring the "slightly lower quality" does not become noticeably inferior. The most important factor is that CP currently pays suppliers after 35 days at fair prices to support Fairtrade producers — the cost savings from Option 2 could actually allow CP to pay the new supplier on delivery, strengthening the relationship while using the remaining savings to fund website development. This means CP could address the online competition threat and improve supplier relationships simultaneously — something Option 1 cannot guarantee. In the short term the Plc route looks more powerful, but in the long term preserving family control, employee loyalty and brand integrity is what will sustain CP for the next 50 years. Therefore, Option 2 is the best way for CP to maintain its current objectives because it protects the values, people and reputation that define CP — while still generating the capital needed to compete.

Exam Technique Masterclass

How to answer every question type for maximum marks
1–2Marks — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase, or single sentence. No explanation needed.
  • Read ALL four options before selecting (MCQs).
  • Use elimination for MCQs.
  • Never write more than one sentence for 1 mark.
2Marks — Explain
Point + development. Two clear sentences. ONE chain of analysis.
  • Rule: marks minus one = number of chains.
  • State the point, then develop using a connective.
4Marks — Explain with context
Point → Explain → Impact. THREE chains of analysis.
  • MUST use case study data where given.
  • Link impact back to the business in the question.
6Marks — Analyse
Point → Evidence → Explain with THREE chains → Link to outcome
  • Use case study evidence in blue.
  • End with a clear outcome for the business.
From 2026, "analyse one reason" now requires TWO reasons/points.
9Marks — Recommend
THREE paragraphs: (1) Argument with 4 chains + data manipulation, (2) Purely evaluative — "more balanced view / further limitation / depends on", (3) Conclusion: "Overall, I believe" + NEW information + short/long term + "Therefore".
  • Para 1: Point → Evidence from case study → Data manipulation → Consequence → Therefore. Minimum 4 chain connectors.
  • Para 2: Purely evaluative — weigh risks and limitations. Use "a more balanced view", "a further limitation", "it also depends on".
  • Para 3: Clear recommendation with NEW information not in paragraphs 1 or 2. Must include short-term vs long-term reasoning. No fence-sitting.
  • Conclusion that only repeats paragraph 1 = Level 2 maximum.
12Marks — Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on". Evaluative paragraph weighing BOTH options. Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on". Para 3: Conclusion with 5 steps.
  • BOTH options: 4 chains each. Manipulate data (calculate savings, percentages, compare figures). End each with "depends on".
  • Separate evaluative section weighing both options against each other — not just "depends on" sentences.
  • Conclusion 5 steps: (1) "To conclude…" (2) "Although… this is outweighed by…" (3) "However, this depends on…" (4) "The most important factor" + NEW information (5) "Therefore…"
  • NEW information MUST appear in the conclusion — a factor, calculation or stakeholder point not raised in paragraphs 1 or 2.
  • Integrate functional areas — link costs → pricing → competitiveness → revenue → profit. Isolated analysis = Level 3 maximum.
