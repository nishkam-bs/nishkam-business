Technology

Spec ref: 3.2.1 · Tested across both papers — e-commerce and digital comms appear every year

E-Commerce: Accessing Wider Markets

E-commerce is buying and selling goods and services over the internet. It allows businesses to sell to customers anywhere in the world, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — without the cost of physical stores.

Benefits of E-Commerce

  • Access to a global market — sell to customers worldwide
  • Open 24/7 — no closing times, sales continue overnight
  • Lower fixed costs — no need for expensive physical stores
  • Easier to compare prices and reach new segments
  • Can target customers using data and personalisation

Drawbacks of E-Commerce

  • Cost of website development and maintenance
  • Security risks — customer payment data can be stolen
  • Cannot physically inspect products before buying
  • Delivery costs and returns management
  • High competition — customers can easily switch to rivals
Case Study · 2022 Paper 1 — Premium Motors
Janet's E-Commerce Decision

Premium Motors developed a new website with photos, videos and live online chat. The 9-mark question asked: Recommend whether Janet should use e-commerce to improve customer service. Prices depended on distance, day and time — complex to display online. However, e-commerce could allow bookings 24/7. Key evaluation: does the complexity of pricing outweigh the benefit of 24/7 accessibility?

Case Study · 2023 Paper 1 — NIST Cars

NIST planned an e-commerce website targeting taxi companies facing city-centre daily charges on petrol cars. Virtual test drives and video features would be used. The 12-mark question evaluated whether this was better than producing a new model in Spain. Key: e-commerce reduces the need for showrooms and opens access to a new B2B market segment.

Model Answer — 6 marks: Analyse how CP benefits from digital technology · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse how CP will benefit from the use of digital technology. [6 marks]

One benefit of CP using digital technology is that it can reduce its labour costs. CP replaced the employees who used to assist shoppers at escalators with interactive screens that display store maps and allow customers to search for products. This means that CP no longer needs to pay wages for these roles across all of its stores. As a result, CP's fixed costs fall, leaving more money available for other areas of the business. Therefore, CP can either reinvest these savings into developing its website to compete with online retailers, or pass the savings on to customers through lower prices. This is particularly significant given that CP is facing increasing competition from online retailers — reducing costs means CP can protect its profit margins without having to raise prices.

Ethical & Environmental Considerations

Spec ref: 3.2.2 · Ethics-profit trade-off tested every year; sustainability is a recurring 4-mark topic

Ethical Behaviour

Ethical behaviour means acting in ways that stakeholders consider to be fair and honest. Ethical businesses go beyond what the law requires — they do what is right, not just what is legal.

Key Definition
  • Business ethics: A set of moral principles and values that guide how a business behaves towards its stakeholders.
  • A business can be legal but still unethical (e.g. paying minimum wage but treating workers poorly).
AreaEthical BehaviourExam Example
SuppliersPaying fair prices; supporting Fairtrade farmers; checking working conditions in supply chainThorntons — Fairtrade cocoa pledge (2019 P1)
EmployeesPaying above minimum wage; never making staff redundant; treating employees as familyCharlie Paul — pays above retail average, never made redundancies in 50 years (2025 P1)
CustomersHonest advertising; fair prices; quality guaranteesBootmills Ltd — 3-month refund policy (2024 P1)
EnvironmentUsing recycled materials; reducing plastic; supporting environmental projectsHelloFresh — recycled packaging (2019 P2); Skyline plc (2022 P2)
CommunitySourcing locally; reducing traffic congestion; supporting local employmentAdventures — only books sustainable hotels (2020 P1)

Benefits of Ethical Behaviour

  • Improved reputation — attracts customers who share similar values
  • Higher employee motivation and loyalty
  • Better supplier relationships — reliable, long-term partnerships
  • Reduced risk of negative press and boycotts
  • Can charge premium prices

Drawbacks (Trade-off with Profit)

  • Higher costs — Fairtrade supplies, recycled materials cost more
  • Lower profit margins
  • May lose customers to cheaper, less ethical rivals
  • Can limit growth — e.g. refusing to use cheap overseas labour
  • Shareholders may pressure business to prioritise profit
Case Study · 2025 Paper 1 — Charlie Paul

It pays suppliers after 35 days at fair prices, supports Fairtrade, pays employees above the national average, and has never made anyone redundant in 50 years. The 12-mark question asked whether CP should convert to a Plc or reduce supply costs — both options threatened CP's ethical objectives. This is the classic ethics–profit trade-off in exam form.

Businesses affect the environment through their operations, transport, waste, and use of resources. The spec requires four specific environmental impacts:

Sustainability means meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Two key concerns:

The Two Sustainability Concerns
  • Global warming: Business activity releases CO₂. Businesses reduce this by switching to renewable energy, using electric vehicles, reducing production waste.
  • Using scarce resources: Raw materials (oil, minerals, forests, water) are finite. Sustainable businesses use recycled materials and reduce consumption.

Model Answer — 6 marks: HelloFresh environmental responsibility · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one benefit to HelloFresh of operating as an environmentally responsible company. [6 marks]

One benefit to HelloFresh of being environmentally responsible is that it can attract more customers. HelloFresh uses recycled packaging and carefully plans its delivery routes to reduce the distance its vehicles travel, and promotes itself as the "greener way to shop for groceries." This means that customers who care about sustainability are more likely to choose HelloFresh over competitors who use non-recycled packaging. As a result, HelloFresh could gain more subscribers, increasing its monthly revenue. Therefore, HelloFresh may be able to charge its premium price of around £5 per person per meal without losing customers — this could lead to stronger profit margins over time. This is particularly valuable given that HelloFresh made net losses in 2017, meaning that attracting loyal, eco-conscious subscribers who stay subscribed long-term is crucial to the business eventually becoming profitable.

Examiner Traps — Ethics & Environment
  • Ethics ≠ always profitable. Always analyse the trade-off between ethics and profit.
  • Sustainability ≠ just recycling. Must link to global warming OR using scarce resources for full marks.
  • Green is for evaluation only. Do not use evaluative language to end a 6-mark analyse answer — end with blue AO2 case study data instead.

The Economic Climate

Spec ref: 3.2.3 · Interest rates tested in 6-mark analysis questions every year (STS 2019, GCC 2025, NIST 2023)

Interest Rates

An interest rate is the cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. Interest rate changes affect both businesses (overdrafts and loans) and consumers (spending power).

ChangeEffect on Business BorrowingEffect on ConsumersImpact on Business Sales
RISE ↑Loan/overdraft repayments more expensive. Less likely to invest.Mortgage repayments rise. Less disposable income. Spend less.Sales of luxury/non-essential items fall. Revenue falls.
FALL ↓Loan/overdraft repayments cheaper. More willing to invest and expand.Mortgage repayments fall. More disposable income. Spend more.Sales increase. Revenue rises. Good time to expand.
Case Study · 2025 Paper 2 — Glam Camping Company (GCC)
Interest Rates & Overdrafts

GCC relies on an overdraft to help with cash flow because festival organisers pay their bill after the festival. When interest rates rise, GCC's overdraft becomes more expensive. The 6-mark question: Analyse one impact that a change in interest rates could have on GCC. Key: always identify whether the business is a borrower (hurt by rate rise) or a saver (helped by rate rise).

Model Answer — 6 marks: Interest rate impact on GCC · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one impact that a change in interest rates could have on GCC. [6 marks]

One impact of rising interest rates on GCC is that its costs will increase. GCC relies on an overdraft to help manage its cash flow, because festival organisers pay their bill after the festival has taken place — meaning GCC often spends money before it receives payment. This means that when interest rates rise, the interest charged on GCC's overdraft increases. As a result, GCC has less cash available to pay its suppliers and staff. Therefore, GCC may need to charge higher prices to its festival clients to cover the extra borrowing cost. This could lead to GCC losing contracts to cheaper competitors who do not rely on an overdraft. This is a serious risk for GCC because the overdraft is not a one-off — it is used repeatedly throughout the year, meaning every rate rise directly reduces the profit GCC makes on each contract it completes.

Examiner Traps — Economic Climate
  • Do NOT explain why interest rates change — just say "interest rates have risen/fallen."
  • Consumer spending ≠ business profits automatically — chain it: spending rises → demand rises → revenue rises → profits rise IF costs stay the same.
  • Interest rates affect SAVERS too — if consumers save more, they spend less, hurting businesses even if the business has no loans.

Exchange Rates

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. Changes affect businesses that import (buy from abroad) and export (sell abroad).

The Golden Rule — No Calculation RequiredYou will NOT be asked to calculate exchange rate conversions.

You only need to analyse the direction of the change and its effect on profit or sales.

Pound ChangeEffect on Exporters (sell abroad)Effect on Importers (buy from abroad)
Pound RISES (stronger £)UK goods become more expensive for foreign buyers → fewer sales abroad → export revenue fallsOverseas goods become cheaper in £ → import costs fall → profits can rise
Pound FALLS (weaker £)UK goods become cheaper for foreign buyers → more exports → export revenue risesOverseas goods become more expensive in £ → import costs rise → profits fall

How to Use This in the Exam — 5 Steps
  • Step 1: Read the exchange rate table in the insert — identify if £ rose or fell
  • Step 2: Identify if the business is an importer, exporter, or both

Case Study · 2024 Paper 1 — Bootmills Ltd
Falling Pound: Impact on Bootmills Exports

Bootmills sells boots to European retailers at an average of £95. The exchange rate fell from £1 = €1.20 (December) to £1 = €1.17 (March). As the pound falls, Bootmills' boots become cheaper for European buyers, which means that European retailers are more likely to buy more boots. This is significant because 60% of Bootmills' sales are abroad — so a small rise in exports has a large impact on total revenue.

Explain one benefit for NIST of a fall in the value of the pound when selling cars in Europe. [4 marks]

One benefit for NIST of a fall in the value of the pound is that its cars become cheaper for European customers to buy. Table 3 shows the exchange rate fell from £1 = €1.10 in March 2019 to £1 = €1.07 in March 2020, which means the euro price of a NIST car fell from €22,000 to €21,400. Model Answer — 6 marks: Falling pound impact on Bootmills profits · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one impact of the falling value of the pound on the profits of Bootmills Ltd. [6 marks]

One impact of the falling pound on Bootmills is that its boots become cheaper for European retailers to buy. The exchange rate fell from £1 = €1.20 in December to £1 = €1.17 in March [a fall of 2.5%], and 60% of Bootmills' sales are made abroad, with approximately half of those in Europe. This means that European retailers now need to spend fewer euros to purchase a pair of Bootmills boots. As a result, Bootmills' handmade boots become more competitively priced compared to rival European boot makers. Therefore, European retailers are more likely to place larger orders, increasing Bootmills' total revenue. This could lead to Bootmills hiring more skilled workers to meet the extra demand. This is particularly significant for Bootmills because international sales make up the majority of its revenue — even a small increase in European demand would have a large positive impact on overall profits, making the falling pound a genuine competitive advantage for Bootmills in the short term.

Markets & Competition

Key Definitions
  • Market: A place (physical or digital) where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods or services.
  • Competition: When two or more businesses try to attract the same customers with similar products or services.

Risks & Uncertainty in Business

Why Entrepreneurs Start Businesses Despite Risk

  • → Financial reward: potential for profit far greater than employed income
  • → Independence: make own decisions, be own boss
  • → Personal satisfaction: building something from nothing, pursuing a passion
  • → Flexible working: control over hours and working pattern
  • → Gap in the market: seeing an opportunity others have not exploited

Activities Businesses Use to Minimise Risk

  • → Market research: find out what customers want before investing
  • → Business planning: forecast costs, revenues, cash flow
  • → Diversification: sell in multiple markets or products to spread risk
  • → Insurance: protects against unexpected losses
  • → Small-scale testing: trial a product before full launch

Interest Rates — 6-Mark Questions

Model Answer — 2025 P2 Q2.2 · 6 marks · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one impact that a change in interest rates could have on GCC. [6 marks]

One impact of an increase in interest rates on GCC is that its costs will rise. GCC uses an overdraft to help manage its cash flow, because festival organisers pay GCC's invoice only after the festival has finished — meaning GCC regularly has to spend money before it receives payment. This means that when interest rates rise, the amount GCC pays in interest on its overdraft increases. As a result, GCC has less profit left over from each contract it completes. Therefore, GCC may have to charge higher prices to festival organisers to cover the increased cost of borrowing. This could lead to GCC losing business to cheaper rivals who do not rely on an overdraft. This is particularly damaging because GCC's overdraft is a regular necessity, not an occasional one — meaning every rate rise reduces GCC's profitability each time it takes on a contract, and the impact compounds over a full season of festivals.

Model Answer — 2019 P2 Q2.2 · 6 marks · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one effect of the increase in interest rates on STS. [6 marks]

One effect of rising interest rates on STS is that Jade's monthly borrowing costs will increase. Jade used a bank loan secured against her own home to start STS, and she uses an overdraft to pay employees at the end of the month when the business does not have enough cash in its account. This means that when interest rates rise, Jade's monthly loan repayment becomes more expensive and the overdraft charges increase. As a result, STS has less cash available to cover day-to-day costs such as rent and equipment. Therefore, Jade may need to increase revenue — for example by increasing lesson prices or taking on more students. This could lead to some existing customers finding STS too expensive and switching to other swim schools. This is an especially serious risk for Jade because the bank loan is secured against her home — if she cannot keep up with higher repayments, she risks not only the business but also losing her house.

Exchange Rates — 4-mark & 6-mark Questions

Model Answer — 2020 P2 Q3.6 · 4 marks

Explain one way that Devine Jewellery will be affected if the exchange rate of the pound falls against other currencies. [4 marks]

If the pound falls, the cost of importing diamonds from India will increase. DJ imports diamonds from India because they are much cheaper to source there than in the UK, which allows DJ to keep its production costs low and charge premium prices on its finished jewellery. This means that when the pound falls, DJ must spend more pounds to buy the same quantity of diamonds from its Indian supplier. As a result, DJ's production costs rise, and the profit margin on each piece of jewellery it sells will fall unless DJ raises its prices to customers.

Environment & Ethics — 6-mark Questions

Model Answer — 2019 P2 Q3.2 · 6 marks · Note: from 2026 this requires TWO developed arguments

Analyse one benefit to HelloFresh of operating as an environmentally responsible company. [6 marks]

One benefit to HelloFresh of being environmentally responsible is that it can attract more customers and increase its revenue. HelloFresh uses fully recycled packaging and plans its delivery routes to minimise the distance its vehicles travel. It promotes itself as the "greener way to shop for groceries" and charges approximately £5 per person per meal. This means that customers who care about the environment are more likely to choose HelloFresh over competitors who use non-recycled packaging. As a result, HelloFresh gains more subscribers, increasing its monthly income. Therefore, HelloFresh is better placed to cover its operating costs, including the cost of sourcing high-quality ingredients and managing its delivery network. This could lead to HelloFresh becoming profitable, which is essential given that it made a net loss in 2017. This is particularly significant because the meal-kit delivery market is becoming increasingly competitive — being known as the environmentally responsible option gives HelloFresh a point of difference that could build the long-term customer loyalty it needs to eventually move from loss into profit.

Exam Technique for Unit 2 Questions

How to answer every question type — from 1 mark to 12 marks

Colour-Coded Answer Writing

Orange = Chain of analysis — This is because… / This means that… / As a result… / Therefore… / This could lead to…
Blue = Case study data (AO2) — business names, figures, facts from insert. Used in ALL 4–12 mark answers.
Green = Evaluation — It depends on… / This is outweighed by… / Overall I believe… — 9-mark and 12-mark ONLY. Never use in 6-mark answers.
1Mark — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase or sentence only. No explanation needed.
  • For MCQ: read all four options before selecting
  • Eliminate obviously wrong answers first
  • Never write more than one sentence
2Marks — Explain
Point + development.
This is because… / This means that… / As a result… / Therefore…
  • Sentence 1: state the point clearly
  • Sentence 2: develop it using an orange connector
  • No evaluation needed — just point + development
4Marks — Explain with Case Study
Point → 3 chains of analysis → Blue AO2 case study data
  • Start with a clear point
  • Build a chain of 3 orange connectors to develop it
  • Use blue case study data — business name, figures, specific details
  • Rule: marks minus 1 = chains needed. 4 marks = 3 chains.
6Marks — Analyse
Open with Blue AO2 → 4 orange chains → Close with Blue AO2
NO green evaluation in 6-mark answers
  • ONE well-developed point (until 2026)
  • Open AND close with blue AO2 case study data — not evaluation
  • Chain 4 orange connectors to build the mechanism
⚡ 2026+: Two separate developed arguments required. Prepare two full points, each with chains and blue AO2 data.
9Marks — Recommend (Weigh up + justify)
Para 1: Argument FOR — 4 orange chains + blue AO2 + "this depends on" | Para 2: Green evaluation — balanced view, limitations | Para 3: Green conclusion — Overall I believe… with NEW reasoning
  • Para 1: "An advantage of [option] is…" → 4 orange chains → end with blue AO2 → "This depends on…" comparing to other option
  • Para 2 & 3 are GREEN — evaluative language only. "It depends on… / A more balanced view… / However… / In the long term…"
  • No fence-sitting — make a clear recommendation and back it up with NEW reasoning in the conclusion
12Marks — Analyse Both Options + Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + blue AO2 | Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + blue AO2 | Para 3: Green evaluation — 5 steps
  • Paras 1 & 2: open and close each with blue AO2 case study data. End with "This depends on [X]…"
  • Para 3 is entirely green evaluative language
12-mark Conclusion — 5 required steps:
1. "To conclude, I think [option] will have the biggest impact… because…"
2. "Although [option 1 point]… this is outweighed by the fact that [option 2 point]…"
3. "However, this depends on… [short term / long term where relevant]"
4. "The most important factor is… [NEW information not mentioned above]"
5. "Therefore, this is why [option] will have the biggest impact on…"

Key Examiner Tips — Unit 2

Common Errors on Unit 2 Questions
  • Exchange rate questions: Always state which direction the rate moved AND identify whether the business imports or exports. Always open and close with blue AO2 data.
  • Interest rate questions: Do NOT explain why rates change. Just say "interest rates rose" and chain the impact.
  • Ethics questions: Do NOT only write benefits. Always acknowledge the trade-off — ethical behaviour costs money.
  • Sustainability ≠ recycling alone. Must link to global warming OR scarce resources for full marks.
  • Green is for evaluation ONLY. 6-mark analyse answers must end with blue AO2 data, not an evaluative judgement.

Exemplar 9-Mark Answer: Should CP convert to a Plc? (2025 P1 Q3.8)

Grade 9 Model — 9-mark Recommend · Orange = chain · Blue = AO2 case study data · Green = evaluation (9/12 mark only)

CP is considering converting to a Plc to raise cash to develop its website. Recommend whether CP should convert to a Plc. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR converting to a Plc:

An advantage of CP converting to a Plc is that it would be able to raise the large amount of capital needed to develop its website. CP's sales have been falling as more customers shop with online retailers, and the business needs funds to build a competitive website that can rival these online competitors. This is because converting to a Plc would allow CP to sell 60% of its shares to the public, raising a significant amount of finance without having to take on a bank loan. This means that CP would not face interest payments on borrowed money, keeping its costs lower. As a result, CP could invest in website development quickly and start recovering lost sales from online competitors. Therefore, CP's revenue could increase as it reaches customers who prefer to shop online, having the biggest impact on CP's ability to protect its market share and grow sales. This depends on whether the money raised from the share sale is enough to build a truly competitive website — if the development costs are higher than expected, the benefit may be limited compared to Option 2.

Paragraph 2 — Balanced evaluation:

However, a more balanced view of this argument is that converting to a Plc carries serious risks for CP that could outweigh the financial benefit. It depends on whether the investors who buy shares share CP's values — and this is far from guaranteed. Potential investor Naia Hayat has stated she would only invest if CP minimises staff costs to maximise profits. This is a fundamental challenge because CP's identity is built on never making staff redundant and treating employees as family — objectives that directly conflict with Naia Hayat's demands. Once CP is a Plc, shareholders have legal rights to influence major decisions, meaning CP could lose control of the very values that have made it trusted and successful for 50 years.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion:

Overall, I believe CP should NOT convert to a Plc. For this to be a success, CP would need to find investors who fully share its ethical objectives — and based on the evidence of Naia Hayat's conditions, this is highly unlikely. This is better than Option 2 (reducing supply costs) because while Option 2 may save money without threatening CP's values, it does not solve the long-term challenge of competing with online retailers. The most important factor to consider is that CP's ethical reputation is its competitive advantage — customers are willing to pay premium prices because they trust CP's values. If converting to a Plc compromises those values, CP could lose the very thing that makes it different. Therefore, CP should not convert to a Plc and should instead explore alternative sources of finance such as a loan that does not require giving up ownership or control.

Exemplar 12-Mark Answer: E-commerce website vs new car model in Spain — NIST (2023 P1 Q3.8)

Grade 9 Model — 12-mark · Orange = chain · Blue = AO2 case study data · Green = evaluation (conclusion only)

NIST wants to increase the number of cars it sells. Analyse Option 1: produce new NIST March in Spain. Analyse Option 2: set up an e-commerce website to sell the Folio electric car to UK businesses. Evaluate which option will have the bigger impact on the number of cars NIST sells. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Produce the new NIST March in Spain

An advantage of NIST producing the new March model in Spain is that it could significantly increase the number of cars NIST sells in Europe. Sales of the Folio model in Europe have been disappointing — NIST sold only 3,200 Folios in Europe in 2022 compared to 18,000 in the UK, and European customers found the Folio's design unsuitable for European driving conditions. This is because the March has been specifically designed for European tastes and road conditions, making it far more likely to appeal to European buyers than the Folio. This means that NIST could win back European customers who previously chose rival brands. As a result, NIST's European sales could increase substantially, adding a significant new revenue stream. Therefore, producing the March in Spain would have the biggest impact on the total number of cars NIST sells globally by opening up a market that is currently underperforming. This depends on whether NIST can maintain its quality standards in the Spanish factory — the UK factory has 5 years without a single fault, and if the March suffers quality problems, this advantage over Option 2 could disappear quickly.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: E-commerce website for UK taxi businesses

An advantage of NIST setting up an e-commerce website to sell the Folio to UK businesses is that it creates an entirely new market segment at lower cost and risk. Many large UK cities are introducing a daily charge for non-electric vehicles entering city centres — taxi companies, which operate their vehicles every day, will face significant additional costs unless they switch to electric cars. NIST plans to allow taxi firms to order the Folio in bulk online, using virtual test drives and video demonstrations. This means that NIST does not need to build new showrooms or hire additional sales staff, keeping the cost of reaching this market low. As a result, NIST can target taxi companies across all major UK cities through a single website. Therefore, even a small number of bulk orders from taxi companies would add a large number of cars to NIST's total sales figures, as companies may order fleets of 20–50 vehicles at a time. This could lead to NIST rapidly increasing its UK sales volume. This depends on how quickly taxi companies actually adopt electric vehicles — if firms delay switching due to the higher upfront cost of the Folio, the e-commerce website may not generate the volume of sales NIST expects.

Paragraph 3 — Evaluative Conclusion

To conclude, I think Option 2 (the e-commerce website) will have the bigger impact on the number of cars NIST sells in the short term. Although the March could open up a large European market, this is outweighed by the fact that the e-commerce website targets an urgent and immediate need — taxi companies are already facing city-centre daily charges and need to act now, meaning demand for the Folio is real and pressing. However, this depends on how many taxi companies can afford the higher upfront cost of electric vehicles. In the long term, the March in Spain could prove more significant as the European market is far larger than the UK taxi sector. The most important factor to consider is the risk to NIST's quality reputation — the business has built its entire brand on having no faults for 5 consecutive years, and if the Spanish-produced March suffers quality issues, the reputational damage would reduce sales of all NIST models. Therefore, Option 2 — the e-commerce website — is the lower-risk choice that will have the bigger impact on cars sold in the short to medium term, while NIST monitors whether the Spanish factory can truly meet its quality standards before committing fully to the March.

Every definition you need to know for the exam
E-commerce
Buying and selling goods and services over the internet, enabling businesses to trade 24/7 and reach customers globally.
Digital Communication
Using electronic technology (social media, websites, email, text, video) to communicate with stakeholders.
Business Ethics
A set of moral principles that guide how a business acts — behaving in ways that stakeholders consider fair and honest.
Ethics–Profit Trade-off
The tension between behaving ethically (which often costs more) and maximising profit (which may require cutting ethical standards).
Sustainability
Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
Global Warming
The rise in Earth's average temperature caused partly by carbon dioxide released by business activity.
Scarce Resources
Raw materials (oil, minerals, timber, water) that are finite. Sustainable businesses reduce their use of scarce resources.
Interest Rate
The cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage of the amount borrowed per year.
Overdraft
A short-term borrowing facility allowing a business to spend more than it has in its bank account, up to an agreed limit.
Consumer Spending
The total amount spent by households on goods and services. Falls when incomes fall or interest rates rise.
Employment Level
The proportion of working-age people who have jobs. High employment → more income → more consumer spending.
Globalisation
The process by which businesses operate and trade internationally, making the world's economies increasingly interconnected.
Exchange Rate
The price of one currency expressed in terms of another. Affects the cost of imports and the competitiveness of exports.
Exporter
A business that sells goods or services to customers in another country. Benefits when its home currency falls (goods become cheaper abroad).
Importer
A business that buys goods or services from another country. Hurt when its home currency falls (imports become more expensive).
Employment Law
Laws protecting employees' rights — includes the National Minimum/Living Wage and the Equality Act 2010.
Equality Act 2010
Law preventing discrimination in employment based on protected characteristics (age, gender, race, disability, religion, etc.).
Health & Safety at Work Act 1974
Law requiring employers to ensure, as far as reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare of all employees.
Consumer Law / Trade Descriptions
Law preventing businesses from making false or misleading statements about products or services.
Market
A place (physical or digital) where buyers and sellers come together to exchange goods or services.
Competition
When two or more businesses try to attract the same customers — creates pressure to improve quality, lower prices and innovate.
Risk
The possibility of something going wrong that can be assessed and planned for.
Uncertainty
Situations where the outcome cannot be predicted — e.g. future exchange rate movements, impact of new technology.
Fairtrade
An ethical trading system that guarantees farmers and producers in developing countries receive a fair minimum price for their goods.
