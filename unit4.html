Organisational Structures

Spec ref: 3.4.1 · Tall vs flat, span of control, delayering and centralisation tested in MCQs and 6-mark questions

What Is Organisational Structure?

An organisational structure is how a business is arranged — who reports to whom, and how information flows. Larger businesses need formal structures; a sole trader needs none. The choice of structure affects communication, decision speed, motivation and cost.

Key Definitions
  • Hierarchy: the layers of authority in a business — CEO at the top, frontline workers at the bottom.
  • Chain of command: the line of authority from the top of the business down to each employee.
  • Span of control: the number of employees who report directly to a single manager.
  • Delegation: passing authority for a task down to a subordinate (while retaining overall responsibility).
  • Delayering: removing a layer of middle management to make the structure flatter.

Tall vs Flat Structures

FeatureTall StructureFlat Structure
HierarchyMany layers of managementFew layers — often only 2 or 3
Span of controlNarrow — each manager supervises a small teamWide — each manager supervises a large team
Chain of commandLong — many steps from top to bottomShort — information travels quickly
Decision-makingSlower — decisions pass through many layersFaster — fewer layers to go through
Staff costHigher — more managers neededLower — fewer managers
Typical exampleArmy, NHS, large banks (HSBC)Tech start-ups, creative agencies, small firms

Tall Structure — Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Clear lines of responsibility and authority.
  • Clear progression routes — employees see how to climb the ladder.
  • Close supervision — problems are spotted quickly.
  • Specialist managers at each level.
  • Clear accountability — everyone knows who to go to.

Disadvantages

  • High management cost — many salaries to pay.
  • Slow decision-making — long chain of command.
  • Poor communication — messages distort as they pass through layers.
  • Lower employee motivation — workers feel distant from decisions.
  • Less flexible — harder to respond quickly to change.

Flat Structure — Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Fast decision-making — short chain of command.
  • Lower management costs — fewer managers needed.
  • Better communication — direct links between senior and junior staff.
  • Higher motivation — employees feel trusted and close to decisions.
  • More flexible — the business can adapt quickly.

Disadvantages

  • Managers may be overloaded — wide span of control is tiring.
  • Less specialist management supervision.
  • Fewer promotion opportunities — no middle ladder to climb.
  • Staff may feel unclear about who to report to.
  • Harder to control in very large businesses.

Centralised vs Decentralised

FeatureCentralisedDecentralised
Who decidesSenior managers at head officeLocal managers closer to the customer
SpeedSlower — decisions pass to the centreFaster — local teams decide on the spot
ConsistencyHigh — same rules everywhereLower — local variations
Local responsivenessLow — one-size-fits-allHigh — each branch adapts
Best suited toBrand-critical retailers (McDonald's, Starbucks)Service businesses (John Lewis Partners, Nordstrom)

Delayering — Why Businesses Do It

Delayering means removing one or more layers of middle management to make the structure flatter and more responsive. It became very popular from the 1990s onwards.

Benefits of Delayering

  • Lower management salary costs.
  • Faster decisions — fewer approval stages.
  • Better communication — direct contact between senior and junior.
  • Empowers junior staff — higher motivation.
  • Forces the business to be leaner and more efficient.

Risks of Delayering

  • Redundancies damage morale for those who remain.
  • Loss of experienced managers and their knowledge.
  • Remaining managers face wider spans of control — stress and overload.
  • Short-term disruption during restructure.
  • Fewer promotion opportunities for juniors in the long term.

Case Study — Valve Corporation (flat structure)

Real-World Example
Valve — Steam, Half-Life and the flat organisation

Valve, the US games company behind Steam and Half-Life, has famously operated with almost no formal hierarchy. Employees choose which projects to work on and can move their desks around the office. The approach works for Valve because the business employs small numbers of very talented, self-motivated people. It would break down in a business with thousands of frontline workers, strict regulatory requirements, or volume manufacturing — which is exactly why tall structures still dominate industries like banking, healthcare and defence.

MCQs — Organisational Structures (Past Papers)

2023 P1 · Q1.41 mark

Which of the following is a benefit of a flat organisational structure?

  • A Close supervision of every worker
  • B Faster decision-making
  • C More layers of management
  • D Narrow span of control
Why B is correctA flat structure has fewer layers, so decisions pass through fewer people — faster. A, C and D all describe tall structures.
Do not confuse "flat" with "small". A huge company (Google in its early years) can still be flat if it has few management layers.
2024 P1 · Q1.51 mark

Which of the following best describes span of control?

  • A The number of layers in a hierarchy
  • B The number of employees who report directly to one manager
  • C The total number of employees in the business
  • D The total number of departments in the business
Why B is correctSpan of control = how many people one manager directly supervises. A is hierarchy depth. C is workforce size. D is departmental structure.
Span is about ONE manager's direct reports — not the whole hierarchy.
Examiner Traps — Structures
  • Flat does not always mean "better". It depends on business size, sector and skill level.
  • Delayering is not the same as redundancy — it specifically removes management layers.
  • Wide span of control belongs to FLAT structures, not tall.
  • Centralised vs decentralised is about where decisions are made, not how many layers there are.

Recruitment & Selection of Employees

Spec ref: 3.4.2 · Internal vs external, job description vs person specification, CV & interviews — tested every year

What Is Recruitment?

Recruitment is the process of finding and hiring the right person for a job. Selection is the stage where shortlisted candidates are assessed and one is chosen. The whole process typically costs a business thousands of pounds, so getting it right matters.

The Stages of Recruitment
  • 1. Identify the vacancy — what role needs filling and why.
  • 2. Write the job description — the duties and responsibilities of the role.
  • 3. Write the person specification — the qualifications, skills and experience needed.
  • 4. Advertise — internally, on the website, job boards (Indeed, LinkedIn), or via a recruitment agency.
  • 5. Receive CVs and application forms — shortlist the best candidates.
  • 6. Interview and test — face-to-face, online, assessment centres, practical tasks.
  • 7. Offer and references — make the offer, take up references, confirm start date.
  • 8. Induction — introduce the new employee to the business.

Internal vs External Recruitment

FeatureInternal RecruitmentExternal Recruitment
Source of candidatePromote or transfer an existing employeeHire someone from outside the business
CostLow — no advertising, no recruitment agencyHigh — adverts, agency fees, interview time
SpeedFast — candidate is already on siteSlow — advertising, interviews, notice period
Knowledge of candidateHigh — track record is already visibleLow — rely on CV and references
New ideas brought inFew — same culture, same thinkingMany — outsider brings fresh perspective
Impact on moralePositive — shows career progression is possibleCan be negative — existing staff feel overlooked

Job Description vs Person Specification

DocumentWhat It DescribesExample Content
Job DescriptionDescribes the JOB — the tasks and responsibilities.Job title, who they report to, daily duties, hours, pay range, location.
Person SpecificationDescribes the PERSON — qualifications, skills, experience and personal qualities.Essential qualifications (e.g. GCSE Maths), desirable qualifications, skills (communication, IT), experience, attributes (reliable, confident).
Easy Way to Remember

Job Description = the JOB. Person Specification = the PERSON. One describes what needs doing; the other describes who can do it.

Methods of Selection

MethodWhat It InvolvesWhen It Works Best
CV / Application FormWritten summary of qualifications and experience submitted by the candidate.First-stage filter — deciding who to invite to interview.
InterviewFace-to-face or online conversation to assess communication, motivation and fit.Almost every role. Often multiple rounds for senior positions.
Psychometric TestStandardised test of personality, reasoning or skill (numerical, verbal, logical).Graduate schemes, professional services, analytical roles.
Practical TestCandidate performs a real task (writing, coding, cooking, driving).Trade roles, creative roles, operational roles.
Assessment CentreGroup exercises, presentations, simulations over one or two days.Graduate schemes, senior management, finance / consulting.
ReferencesContact with a previous employer or teacher to confirm character and track record.Final verification before an offer is confirmed.

The Cost of a Bad Hire

Why Recruitment Matters
  • Recruiting a replacement typically costs 20–30% of the salary in fees, training and lost productivity.
  • A bad hire damages team morale — colleagues pick up extra work and frustration grows.
  • Customer-facing bad hires can damage the brand quickly.
  • Training investment is lost if the new hire leaves within 12 months.
  • Legal disputes over dismissal can lead to tribunal claims and reputational damage.

Case Study — Greggs

Real-World Example
Greggs — recruitment at the heart of growth

Greggs, the UK bakery chain, employs more than 27,000 people across 2,500 shops. It uses a mix of internal recruitment (promoting shop assistants to shop managers, and managers to area managers) and external recruitment for specialist roles in head office. Every new shop assistant goes through a structured induction and receives ongoing customer service training. Greggs has consistently been named one of the best companies to work for in UK retail — a direct consequence of strong recruitment, fair pay and training practices.

Motivating Employees

Spec ref: 3.4.3 · Financial vs non-financial motivation and Maslow/Taylor/Herzberg applications tested almost every year

What Is Motivation?

Motivation is what drives an employee to work hard, care about the business and stay. A motivated workforce is productive, loyal and quick to solve problems. A demotivated workforce absents itself, cuts corners and leaves — costing the business dearly.

Why Motivation Matters
  • Higher productivity — motivated workers produce more per hour.
  • Better quality — they care about what they do.
  • Lower staff turnover — saves recruitment and training costs.
  • Stronger customer service — happy staff make happy customers.
  • Fewer disputes — less absence, fewer grievances, fewer strikes.

Financial Methods of Motivation

MethodHow It WorksBest Suited To
WagePaid per hour worked. Overtime available. Flexible but income varies week to week.Part-time retail, hospitality, temporary workers.
SalaryFixed annual amount paid monthly. Predictable income, no direct link to hours worked.Permanent professional roles (managers, teachers, nurses).
CommissionA percentage of sales made — the more you sell, the more you earn.Estate agents, car salespeople, recruitment consultants.
BonusOne-off payment for hitting a target (individual, team or company).Bankers, sales teams, senior executives.
Profit-SharingA share of the company's annual profit paid to employees.John Lewis Partnership, some law and consulting firms.
Piece-RatePaid per unit produced — encourages high output, risks quality.Factory workers, garment manufacturing, farm workers.
Fringe BenefitsNon-cash extras: pension, healthcare, staff discount, company car.Larger corporate employers, financial services.

Non-Financial Methods of Motivation

MethodWhat It MeansImpact
Job RotationMoving an employee through different tasks or roles.Reduces boredom, builds variety of skills, covers absence.
Job EnrichmentAdding more demanding, skilled, responsible tasks to a role.Makes work more meaningful; grows the employee; retains talent.
Job EnlargementAdding more tasks of the same level (horizontal expansion).Reduces monotony but can feel like "more work for the same pay".
Empowerment & AutonomyGiving employees authority to make decisions.Raises motivation, speeds decision-making, develops leaders.
TeamworkOrganising work in teams that are jointly accountable.Builds belonging, shared success, peer support.
Recognition & PraisePublic thanks, awards, "employee of the month".Free but powerful — people want to feel seen.
Flexible WorkingRemote, part-time, compressed hours, job-share.Huge post-COVID driver of engagement, especially for parents.
Training & DevelopmentInvesting in employees' skills and careers.Builds loyalty, raises capability, reduces turnover.

Theories of Motivation (Brief Overview)

Taylor (1911)

ViewPeople work mainly for money.
MethodPiece-rate pay — the more you produce, the more you earn.
LimitIgnores non-financial needs — recognition, purpose, growth.

Maslow (1943)

ViewPeople have a hierarchy of needs — physical, safety, social, esteem, self-actualisation.
MethodMeet basic needs first (fair pay), then build up (recognition, growth).
UseDesigning total reward packages.

Herzberg (1959)

ViewTwo-factor theory: hygiene factors (pay, conditions) prevent dissatisfaction; motivators (recognition, growth) drive satisfaction.
ImplicationPaying more alone will not motivate — you must also enrich the work itself.
UseJob enrichment, empowerment, meaningful feedback.
How to Use Theories in Answers

In a 9-mark or 12-mark answer, reference a theory briefly to show depth: "According to Herzberg, pay alone prevents dissatisfaction but does not motivate — job enrichment is needed for genuine engagement." One sentence is enough.

Case Study — John Lewis Partnership

Real-World Example
John Lewis Partnership — every employee is a Partner

At John Lewis and Waitrose, every employee is a Partner and receives a share of the annual profit. In good years this has been as much as 15–18% of annual salary, effectively an extra two months' pay. Beyond the bonus, Partners have a voice in how the business is run through democratically elected councils. The result has historically been lower staff turnover, stronger customer service, and one of the UK's most respected retail cultures. In recent years, as profits have fallen, the Partnership bonus has been paused — a reminder that profit-sharing cuts both ways.

Training

Spec ref: 3.4.4 · Induction, on-the-job vs off-the-job training — tested in 4-mark and 6-mark questions

What Is Training?

Training is the process of teaching employees the skills and knowledge they need to do their job well. Businesses that invest in training tend to have more productive, more loyal and more capable workforces. Businesses that skip training tend to have higher staff turnover, more mistakes and weaker customer service.

Why Businesses Train
  • New employees need to learn the job (induction).
  • Existing employees need to learn new technology, processes or products.
  • Legal requirements (health and safety, food hygiene, data protection).
  • Career progression — preparing employees for promotion.
  • Raising quality, reducing errors, improving customer service.
  • Attracting and retaining the best employees.

Types of Training

TypeWhat It IsExamples
InductionInitial training given to new employees to introduce them to the business and their role.Office tour, meeting the team, health & safety briefing, first training sessions.
On-the-JobTraining delivered while the employee is doing the real job, often alongside a colleague.Shadowing a senior, coaching from a line manager, learning by doing.
Off-the-JobTraining delivered away from the normal workplace, often by external trainers.College course, professional qualification, away-day training seminar.
Ongoing (Continuous Professional Development)Training that continues throughout an employee's career.Annual first-aid refresh, new software training, leadership development programmes.

On-the-Job vs Off-the-Job Training

On-the-Job — Strengths

  • Cheaper — no external trainer fees.
  • Directly relevant to the actual job.
  • Employee is still productive during training.
  • Builds relationships with experienced colleagues.
  • Immediate feedback on real tasks.

On-the-Job — Weaknesses

  • Trainer (senior colleague) is distracted from their own work.
  • Bad habits can be passed on.
  • Limited to the skills present inside the business.
  • Interruptions from real work can disrupt learning.
  • Harder to assess if competence has been reached.

Off-the-Job — Strengths

  • Access to specialist external trainers and qualifications.
  • Focused learning environment — no interruptions.
  • Brings in new ideas and best practice from outside.
  • Valuable certified qualifications (e.g. food hygiene, first aid).
  • Can train many employees at once.

Off-the-Job — Weaknesses

  • Expensive — trainer fees, travel, venue hire.
  • Employee is not productive during the training.
  • Content may be generic — not specific to the business.
  • Risk that the employee leaves after completing the qualification.
  • Transferring classroom learning to real work is not always easy.

Why Induction Matters

A new employee's first few days and weeks set the tone for their whole career at the business. A good induction:

The Ingredients of Great Induction
  • Welcomes the new employee warmly from the moment they arrive.
  • Introduces them to their team, their manager and key colleagues.
  • Covers health & safety, IT, policies and essential training.
  • Gives them real work — but realistic for week one.
  • Sets clear goals for the first 30, 60 and 90 days.
  • Includes regular check-ins from the line manager.

The Training vs Recruitment Trade-Off

SituationTrain Existing StaffRecruit New Staff
Loyal workforce in placeBetter option — builds career progression and loyalty.Risks demotivating existing staff who feel overlooked.
Urgent skill gapToo slow — training takes months.Better option — hire the skills immediately.
Budget is tightTraining is typically cheaper than recruitment.Higher cost: advertising, agency fees, notice period delays.
Brand-new capability neededDifficult — no existing skills to build on.Better option — bring expertise from outside.

Case Study — McDonald's Hamburger University

Real-World Example
McDonald's — Hamburger University

McDonald's runs its own global training academy, Hamburger University, which has trained more than 300,000 managers since it opened in 1961. The programme delivers consistent standards across thousands of restaurants in more than 100 countries. The business has argued for decades that its training investment — both on-the-job in restaurants and off-the-job at the University — is the single most important reason for the consistent customer experience worldwide. McDonald's has also been recognised repeatedly as one of the UK's largest providers of apprenticeships.

9-Mark Exemplar: Should BrightCare Ltd delayer its management?

Case Study — BrightCare Ltd (practice item)

BrightCare Ltd runs 18 care homes across the Midlands. It has five management layers between the Managing Director and the frontline carers. Staff turnover is 34% a year — well above the industry average. An employee survey showed that 62% of carers feel their ideas are never heard. The MD is considering removing one management layer to save £420,000 a year in salaries and shorten the chain of command.

Model 9-Mark Answer

Q: Recommend whether BrightCare Ltd should delayer its management. Give reasons for your recommendation. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR delayering (4 chains + data):
An advantage of delayering is that BrightCare Ltd would cut significant costs while shortening its chain of command. Removing one management layer would save £420,000 a year in salaries. This is because fewer managers means fewer senior salaries to pay, and decisions can travel faster from the MD to the frontline carers without passing through so many approval stages. This means that BrightCare Ltd could reinvest the £420,000 in improving pay and training for the frontline carers — directly addressing the reason 34% of staff leave every year. As a result, ideas from the ground would reach senior managers faster — which could help fix the finding that 62% of carers feel their ideas are never heard. Therefore, delayering could have the biggest impact on both cost savings and staff engagement simultaneously.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view would recognise that delayering has significant human costs. This suggests that redundancies among middle managers could damage morale across the remaining workforce, many of whom would fear they could be next — possibly increasing the already-high 34% turnover in the short term. A further limitation is that remaining managers would face wider spans of control, supervising larger teams of carers in 18 separate homes across the Midlands. It also depends on how the change is communicated — if the delayering is announced badly, the organisation could lose experienced managers who have decades of regulatory and operational knowledge crucial in the highly regulated care sector.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe BrightCare Ltd should delayer — but only one layer and with careful, transparent communication. Although redundancies are painful and disrupt morale, this is outweighed by the opportunity to invest £420,000 a year into better carer pay and training — the single biggest driver of the 34% turnover. However, this depends on whether BrightCare Ltd can redeploy some affected managers into new frontline leadership roles to retain their regulatory knowledge. The most important factor is that care homes are regulated by the Care Quality Commission, and inspection outcomes depend heavily on the stability and morale of frontline carers — so reinvesting in carers has a direct impact on regulatory ratings, reputation and future funding. In the short term delayering will be disruptive as redundancies are managed, but in the long term the lower turnover, improved pay and stronger frontline voice will build a more stable, better-regulated business. Therefore, BrightCare Ltd should delayer carefully, reinvesting the savings in the carers themselves.

12-Mark Exemplar: Pay rise or job enrichment — which motivates more?

Case Study — Orbit Logistics Ltd (practice item)
Orbit Logistics Ltd

Orbit Logistics Ltd employs 280 warehouse workers across three sites in the North West. Staff turnover is 28% a year. A recent survey found that 74% of staff are "bored" and 51% feel the work is repetitive. The Operations Director is choosing between two options.
Option 1 — Pay rise: Increase hourly pay by 8%. Estimated cost £520,000 a year. Competitors in the region already pay similar or higher rates, so the pay rise would mainly bring Orbit level rather than give a competitive edge.
Option 2 — Job enrichment: Redesign warehouse roles into small self-managed teams responsible for quality, planning and customer feedback. Cost £180,000 in training, then £60,000 a year in ongoing support. Competitors using this model report staff turnover below 12% within two years.

Model 12-Mark Answer

Q: Evaluate which of these two options will have the biggest impact on Orbit Logistics Ltd's staff motivation and business performance. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Pay rise (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of an 8% pay rise is that Orbit Logistics Ltd would reduce the immediate financial reason for staff to leave. The pay rise costs £520,000 a year. This is because according to Maslow, safety and physiological needs (including fair income) sit at the bottom of the hierarchy — until those are met, higher motivators do not work. This means that staff who leave purely because rival employers pay more would now have less financial reason to go, reducing the 28% annual turnover. As a result, recruitment and training costs — typically 20–30% of salary per replacement — would fall, partially offsetting the £520,000 pay bill. Therefore, the pay rise could have an immediate impact on stopping the cash-driven resignations. However, this depends on how customers and investors respond to the higher staff costs — and on whether competitors respond by matching the rise, cancelling out the advantage.

Evaluation — Option 1:
A more balanced assessment is that pay alone does not address the real problem. This is significant because 74% of staff are bored and 51% feel the work is repetitive — these are Herzberg's motivators, not hygiene factors. Fixing pay will stop some leaving, but the boredom problem will remain.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: Job enrichment (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of job enrichment is that Orbit Logistics Ltd would address the underlying cause of the turnover. Competitors using this model report turnover below 12% within two years. This is because redesigning roles into self-managed teams with responsibility for quality, planning and customer feedback tackles the exact issues staff reported: boredom and repetition. This means that frontline workers gain variety, responsibility and a sense of ownership — the motivators Herzberg identified as genuinely driving satisfaction. As a result, turnover could fall from 28% to near the 12% reported by competitors, potentially saving well over £1 million a year in recruitment and retraining costs for 280 staff. Therefore, job enrichment could have the biggest impact on both motivation and long-term cost structure. However, this depends on whether Orbit's managers can genuinely let go of control — empowerment only works where leaders trust their teams.

Evaluation — Option 2:
A limitation is that job enrichment takes time to embed. Competitors needed two years to see the 12% turnover — meaning Orbit would keep paying replacement costs during the transition. It also depends on whether frontline staff actually want the extra responsibility — some may prefer a simpler role.

Conclusion — 5 Steps:
To conclude, I think that job enrichment will have the biggest impact on Orbit Logistics Ltd's motivation and business performance. Although the 8% pay rise is faster and simpler, this is outweighed by the fact that pay is only a hygiene factor in Herzberg's terms — it stops dissatisfaction but does not generate genuine motivation. The real problem is boredom, and enrichment addresses it directly. However, this depends on whether Orbit Logistics Ltd can fund the £180,000 upfront training cost while turnover is already eroding profit — possibly by phasing the change across one site first to prove the model before rolling it out to all three. The most important factor is that warehouse operations rely heavily on customer service ratings with retailer clients; Amazon-style same-day expectations mean engaged, self-managed teams directly drive the delivery accuracy and speed that retains clients. In the short term the pay rise looks easier and more visible, but in the long term enrichment transforms the cost structure (far lower turnover) and the client proposition (better service). Therefore, job enrichment is the best option because it fixes the root cause of Orbit's problem rather than just raising the cost base.

Exam Technique Masterclass

How to answer every question type for maximum marks
1–2Marks — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase, or single sentence. No explanation needed.
  • Read ALL four options before selecting (MCQs).
  • Use elimination for MCQs.
  • Never write more than one sentence for 1 mark.
2Marks — Explain
Point + development. Two clear sentences. ONE chain of analysis.
  • Rule: marks minus one = number of chains.
  • State the point, then develop using a connective.
4Marks — Explain with context
Point → Explain → Impact. THREE chains of analysis.
  • MUST use case study data where given.
  • Link impact back to the business in the question.
6Marks — Analyse
Point → Evidence → Explain with THREE chains → Link to outcome
  • Use case study evidence in blue.
  • End with a clear outcome for the business.
From 2026, "analyse one reason" now requires TWO developed reasons/points.
9Marks — Recommend
THREE paragraphs: (1) Argument with 4 chains + data manipulation, (2) Purely evaluative — "more balanced view / further limitation / depends on", (3) Conclusion: "Overall, I believe" + NEW information + short/long term + "Therefore".
  • Para 1: Point → Evidence from case study → Data manipulation → Consequence → Therefore. Minimum 4 chain connectors.
  • Para 2: Purely evaluative — weigh risks and limitations.
  • Para 3: Clear recommendation with NEW information not in paragraphs 1 or 2. Must include short-term vs long-term reasoning. No fence-sitting.
  • Conclusion that only repeats paragraph 1 = Level 2 maximum.
12Marks — Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 3: Conclusion with 5 steps.
  • BOTH options: 4 chains each. Manipulate data (calculate savings, percentages, compare figures).
  • Each option followed by a short concise evaluation (2–3 sentences).
  • Conclusion 5 steps: (1) "To conclude…" (2) "Although… this is outweighed by…" (3) "However, this depends on…" (4) "The most important factor" + NEW information (5) "Therefore…"
  • NEW information MUST appear in the conclusion — a factor, calculation or stakeholder point not raised in paragraphs 1 or 2.
  • Integrate functional areas — link HR → productivity → quality → customers → profit. Isolated analysis = Level 3 maximum.

Key Terms Glossary — Unit 4

Every definition you need to know for the exam
Organisational Structure
How a business is arranged — who reports to whom, and how information flows through the hierarchy.
Hierarchy
The layers of authority in a business, from the most senior leader at the top to frontline workers at the bottom.
Chain of Command
The line of authority that runs from the top of an organisation down to each employee.
Span of Control
The number of employees who report directly to a single manager.
Tall Structure
A hierarchy with many layers of management and narrow spans of control. Long chain of command.
Flat Structure
A hierarchy with few layers of management and wide spans of control. Short chain of command.
Delayering
Removing one or more layers of management to make the structure flatter and reduce costs.
Delegation
Passing authority for a task down to a subordinate, while retaining overall responsibility.
Centralised
A structure where decisions are made by senior managers at head office.
Decentralised
A structure where decision-making is pushed down to local branches or teams.
Recruitment
The process of finding and hiring a new employee for a role.
Selection
The stage of recruitment where candidates are assessed (interviews, tests) and one is chosen.
Internal Recruitment
Filling a vacancy by promoting or transferring an existing employee.
External Recruitment
Filling a vacancy by hiring someone from outside the business.
Job Description
A document describing the tasks, responsibilities and conditions of a particular job.
Person Specification
A document describing the qualifications, skills, experience and qualities the ideal candidate should have.
CV (Curriculum Vitae)
A written summary of a candidate's qualifications, experience and skills.
Induction
The initial training given to a new employee to introduce them to the business and their role.
On-the-Job Training
Training delivered while the employee is doing the real job, often alongside a colleague.
Off-the-Job Training
Training delivered away from the normal workplace, often by external trainers.
Motivation
What drives an employee to work hard, care about the business and stay.
Wage
Payment based on hours worked — varies week to week.
Salary
A fixed annual amount paid monthly regardless of hours worked.
Commission
A percentage of sales paid to the employee — directly linked to individual performance.
Bonus
A one-off payment made for hitting a target (individual, team or company).
Profit-Sharing
A share of the company's annual profit paid to employees.
Piece-Rate
Payment based on the number of units produced.
Fringe Benefits
Non-cash extras such as pension, healthcare, staff discount or a company car.
Job Rotation
Moving an employee between different tasks or roles to reduce boredom and build skills.
Job Enrichment
Adding more demanding, skilled or responsible tasks to a role to raise motivation.
Job Enlargement
Adding more tasks of the same level to a role (horizontal expansion).
Empowerment
Giving employees the authority to make decisions about their own work.

The virtues Nishkam builds in you — integrity, compassion, justice, humility — are exactly what AI cannot replicate. Salary Growth — What Makes the Difference?
CIPD qualification (Chartered Institute of Personnel & Development) can add £5,000–£15,000 per year. Chartered Fellow status is the gold standard.
London & South East premium — HR salaries are typically 15–25% higher than national average for equivalent roles.
Sector matters — financial services and tech HR pay significantly more than charity or public sector equivalents.
Data & analytics skills — HR professionals who can use Power BI, SQL or people analytics platforms earn premiums of 10–20% in 2025.
