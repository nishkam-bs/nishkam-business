The Nishkam Ethos

At Nishkam, finance is never just about numbers. Every figure in a set of accounts is the result of human choices — choices about honesty in reporting, fairness to suppliers, diligence in record-keeping and determination to keep a business alive when cash is tight. The virtues we cultivate in school are the exact qualities society most needs in those who manage its money.

Faith-Inspired
Money is a tool, not a master. Every business decision has an ethical dimension, and wealth carries responsibility — to employees, suppliers, customers and community.
Virtues-Led
Honesty in the cash flow forecast. Diligence in break-even analysis. Determination to act when margins fall. Finance rewards the virtuous habits we build each day in Form time.
Aspiring for Excellence
We prepare you not just to pass the paper but to handle real money with skill — whether in your own business, a corporate finance role, or the family budget.
Honesty in Reporting
Cash flow forecasts, profit calculations and financial statements are only useful if they are truthful. A business that inflates its revenue or hides costs is building a future on sand — and hurting every stakeholder who trusts its figures.

Paying suppliers fairly and on time is a moral choice, not only a commercial one. Using trade credit to delay payment indefinitely strangles small businesses that depend on you — Nishkam insists finance is practised with conscience.

A Word Before You Begin

The most dangerous mistake in finance is mistaking profit for cash. A business can show a profit on paper and still be unable to pay its staff next week — because customers have not yet paid their invoices. Unit 6 trains you to spot the warning signs early, plan calmly, and act with both courage and caution. These are not exam skills alone. They are life skills.

Behaviour for Learning — The Three Virtues

Peacefulness · Diligence · Determination — at the heart of every lesson
Peacefulness

Peacefulness is the stillness that allows focused, undistracted learning. A peaceful classroom is one where every voice can be heard, where disagreements are handled with calm, and where the mind is free to think deeply.

In Business Studies: Peacefulness means reading questions carefully before responding, approaching difficult topics with an open mind, and listening fully to others before forming your own view.
Diligence

Diligence is careful, persistent effort. It is the willingness to check your work, to revise what you already know, and to do small things well repeatedly rather than big things badly once.

In Business Studies: Diligence means building strong subject foundations through consistent practice, noticing small distinctions between key terms, and rewriting answers that are almost good enough until they are fully right.
Determination

Determination is the resolve to keep going when learning is difficult. It is the refusal to give up on a problem simply because the answer is hard to reach.

In Business Studies: Determination means pushing through to complete chains of analysis rather than stopping halfway, and holding a clear position in evaluation questions even when the topic is complex.

3.6.1

Sources of Finance

Internal and external sources — matching the source to the need

Why Businesses Need Finance

Every business, at every stage, needs money to operate. Start-ups need finance to buy equipment, rent premises and launch products. Established businesses need finance to expand, cover cash flow gaps, replace ageing assets or survive a downturn. The challenge is not just finding money — it is choosing the right kind of money for the purpose.

Key Distinction — Short-term vs Long-term Finance

Short-term finance funds day-to-day operating needs (stock, wages, bills) and is typically repaid within a year — examples include overdrafts and trade credit. Long-term finance funds major investments (buildings, machinery, acquisitions) and is typically repaid over several years — examples include mortgages, long-term loans and share capital.

Internal Sources of Finance

Internal finance comes from within the business itself — no outside lender or investor is required. This protects control and avoids interest, but is limited by what the business has already earned or owns.

SourceWhat It IsBest Used ForLimitation
Owner's CapitalMoney the business owner personally invests — from savings or personal assets.Start-up funding; small ongoing investments.Limited by what the owner has. Risks personal wealth.
Retained ProfitProfit left over after tax and dividends, kept in the business for reinvestment.Expansion, replacing assets, building a buffer.Only available to profitable, established businesses. Takes time to accumulate.
Selling AssetsSelling equipment, vehicles, property or other assets no longer needed.Raising a one-off sum without taking on debt.Can only be used once per asset. May harm future operations if the wrong asset is sold.

External Sources of Finance

External finance comes from outside the business. It allows access to larger sums, but usually involves interest, repayment obligations, loss of control or all three.

SourceWhat It IsBest Used ForLimitation
OverdraftA short-term borrowing facility from a bank that lets a business spend more than is in its account, up to an agreed limit.Covering short-term cash gaps between paying suppliers and receiving customer payments.Very high interest rates. Bank can demand repayment at any time.

Venture CapitalInvestment from professional investors (often for a large equity stake) in high-growth businesses.Fast-growing start-ups with large potential, especially in tech.Investors demand significant control and often a board seat. High performance pressure.
CrowdfundingRaising small amounts from many people online — can be donation, reward, equity or loan-based.Consumer-facing start-ups, creative projects, products with a community.No guarantee of success. Public failure if target not reached.

Choosing the Right Source — Key Factors

Factors Pushing Toward Internal Finance

  • Business has strong retained profit to use
  • Owner wants full control retained
  • Amount needed is relatively small
  • Interest rates are high or credit is tight
  • Avoiding debt is a strategic priority

Factors Pushing Toward External Finance

  • Large amount needed quickly (expansion, acquisition)
  • Business does not yet have profits (start-up)
  • Long payback period acceptable (e.g. mortgage for premises)
  • Growth ambition requires outside expertise (venture capital)
  • Government support available (grants)
Real-World Example
Innocent Drinks — From Market Stall to £100m Sale

When Richard Reed, Adam Balon and Jon Wright launched Innocent Drinks in 1999, they put up a sign at a music festival asking customers to vote on whether they should quit their jobs to make smoothies full-time. With owner's capital of just £500 between them, they needed external finance to scale. After bank rejections, they raised £250,000 from an American angel investor — venture capital — in exchange for a significant equity stake. By 2013, Coca-Cola had bought the business for an estimated £100 million. The trade-off was clear: without the venture capital they could not have scaled, but accepting it set the path to eventual sale rather than independent growth.

MCQ — 3.6.1: Which of the following is an internal source of finance?
  • A) Bank loan
  • B) Retained profit
  • C) Venture capital
  • D) Overdraft
Answer: B — Retained profit is money the business has already earned, kept within the business for reinvestment. All other options come from external lenders or investors.
Past Paper Question — 9 marks

Q: Recommend whether a start-up mobile coffee van business should finance its £45,000 setup cost through a bank loan or venture capital. Give reasons for your recommendation.

Hint: A mobile coffee van has limited growth ambition — not "the next Deliveroo". Venture capitalists want high-growth, scalable tech-style businesses; they would rarely invest in a single-van operation. A bank loan is far more realistic — it preserves ownership, has predictable repayments, and suits an owner-operated business. Include what happens if sales are slow in winter (a key cash-flow risk for a seasonal business).
Examiner Trap: Students often confuse share capital (selling shares for cash) with share dividends (paying shareholders out of profit). Share capital is a source of finance; dividends are a use of finance. Also be careful not to describe a sole trader as using "share capital" — sole traders cannot issue shares.
3.6.2

Cash Flow

Inflows, outflows, forecasts and why cash is different from profit

What Is Cash Flow?

Cash flow is the movement of money into and out of a business over a period of time. Cash flowing in (inflows) comes from customer payments, loans received, asset sales and investments. Cash flowing out (outflows) goes to suppliers, wages, rent, utilities, loan repayments and tax. Net cash flow for the period = total inflows − total outflows.

The Single Most Important Distinction in Finance

Cash is not the same as profit. A business can be profitable on paper (selling more than it costs to produce) but run out of cash if customers pay late or stock ties up money. More UK businesses fail from running out of cash than from being unprofitable. This is why cash flow forecasting matters more than almost any other financial skill.

Typical Inflows and Outflows

Cash Inflows

  • Sales revenue — payments from customers (the most important inflow)
  • Loans received — money from a bank or lender
  • Owner investment — capital put in by the owner
  • Sale of assets — one-off cash from selling equipment or property
  • Grants received — from government or charities
  • Interest received — on business savings

Cash Outflows

  • Payments to suppliers — for stock and raw materials
  • Wages and salaries — payroll each month
  • Rent and utilities — premises and energy
  • Loan repayments — principal + interest
  • Tax payments — VAT, corporation tax, PAYE
  • Capital expenditure — new equipment, vehicles, fit-out

The Cash Flow Forecast

A cash flow forecast is a prediction of all the inflows and outflows for each month ahead — usually for 6 or 12 months. It allows a business to spot cash shortages before they arrive and take action. Every business plan presented to a bank contains a cash flow forecast.

How to Read a Cash Flow Forecast

Net cash flow = total inflows − total outflows for that month. Opening balance = the closing balance from the previous month. Closing balance = opening balance + net cash flow. A negative closing balance means the business cannot pay its bills — a cash crisis is arriving.

Real-World Example
Carillion — A Profitable Business That Collapsed

Carillion was a major UK construction and services company with contracts across the NHS, schools and government departments. On paper, it reported profits and paid dividends to shareholders. But its cash flow told a different story: it was paying suppliers 120 days after receiving their work while customers paid it much later still. When the gap became too wide, Carillion could not meet its own wage and supplier obligations. In January 2018 it went into compulsory liquidation, owing nearly £7 billion and making 3,000 employees redundant. The lesson: profit on paper does not pay wages next Friday — cash does.

MCQ — 3.6.2: A business has total inflows of £45,000, total outflows of £52,000 and an opening balance of £10,000. What is its closing balance?
  • A) £3,000
  • B) £7,000
  • C) £17,000
  • D) –£7,000
Answer: A — Net cash flow = £45,000 − £52,000 = −£7,000. Closing balance = £10,000 + (−£7,000) = £3,000.
Past Paper Question — 6 marks

Q: Analyse two ways a seasonal garden centre could improve its cash flow during quiet winter months. (2026 format: TWO developed arguments required.)

Hint: Strong options include arranging an overdraft with the bank before winter starts (predictable, fast, but interest-heavy); diversifying the product range into Christmas decorations or indoor plants (adds inflows during the dead months); offering pre-paid gift vouchers for spring (converts future sales into immediate cash). Each argument needs evidence and a clear chain of analysis leading to the business outcome.
Examiner Trap: Students often write "the business is making a loss" when they see a negative net cash flow. This is wrong. A negative net cash flow for a single month is normal (wages paid in month 1, customer revenue received in month 2). A loss is an accounting concept over a whole period where costs exceed revenue. Cash-flow timing is not the same as profitability.

Financial Terms & Calculations

Revenue, costs, profit, break-even and margin of safety

The Key Financial Terms

TermDefinitionFormula
Revenue (Sales / Turnover)Total money received from sales before any costs are deducted.Price per unit × Quantity sold
Fixed CostsCosts that do not change with output — e.g. rent, management salaries, insurance.Fixed regardless of units sold
Variable CostsCosts that rise and fall with output — e.g. raw materials, packaging, piece-rate wages.Variable cost per unit × Quantity
Total CostsAll costs the business incurs in a period.Fixed Costs + Variable Costs
Gross ProfitProfit after deducting the direct cost of making the product.Revenue − Cost of Sales
Net ProfitProfit after deducting ALL costs (including overheads, interest and tax).Gross Profit − Expenses (& Tax)
Break-Even PointThe level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs — the business makes neither a profit nor a loss.Fixed Costs ÷ (Price − Variable Cost per unit)
Margin of SafetyThe difference between actual (or forecast) sales and break-even sales. Shows how far sales can fall before the business makes a loss.Actual Sales − Break-Even Sales

Worked Example — Break-Even & Margin of Safety

Worked Example — BurgerStop Ltd

BurgerStop Ltd sells burgers at £6 each. Each burger costs £2 in raw materials (variable cost). Fixed costs (rent, salaries, utilities) are £8,000 per month. The business currently sells 2,500 burgers per month.

Step 1 — Contribution per unit: £6 − £2 = £4 per burger.
Step 2 — Break-even in units: £8,000 ÷ £4 = 2,000 burgers.
Step 3 — Break-even in revenue: 2,000 × £6 = £12,000.
Step 4 — Margin of safety: 2,500 − 2,000 = 500 burgers (or £3,000 in revenue).

What This Means: BurgerStop covers its costs at 2,000 burgers per month. Every burger sold beyond that produces £4 of pure profit. Sales can fall by 500 burgers (20%) before the business starts losing money — a reasonable, but not huge, safety cushion.

Profit Margins

MeasureFormulaWhat It Tells You
Gross Profit Margin (%)(Gross Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100How much of every £1 of sales is left after the direct cost of making the product. A high margin indicates strong pricing power or low production costs.
Net Profit Margin (%)(Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100How much of every £1 of sales is left after ALL costs. The true measure of profitability; can be squeezed by high overheads even when gross margin is healthy.
Worked Example

A business has revenue of £500,000, cost of sales £300,000, expenses £120,000.
Gross profit = £500,000 − £300,000 = £200,000. Gross profit margin = £200,000 ÷ £500,000 × 100 = 40%.
Net profit = £200,000 − £120,000 = £80,000. Net profit margin = £80,000 ÷ £500,000 × 100 = 16%.

Real-World Example
Greggs — The Power of a Small Margin, High Volume

Greggs has a net profit margin of around 6–8% — lower than luxury brands like Burberry, which can exceed 20%. But Greggs sells over 5 million items per week across 2,500 shops. The business demonstrates that a small margin on massive volume can beat a huge margin on small volume. A change of 1 percentage point in its gross profit margin — caused by wheat or energy prices — can wipe out millions in annual profit. This is why margin discipline is so central to retail finance.

MCQ — 3.6.3: A business sells a product for £20. Variable costs per unit are £12. Fixed costs are £16,000. What is the break-even point in units?
  • A) 800 units
  • B) 1,333 units
  • C) 2,000 units
  • D) 16,000 units
Answer: C — Contribution per unit = £20 − £12 = £8. Break-even = £16,000 ÷ £8 = 2,000 units.
Past Paper Question — 4 marks

Q: A business has fixed costs of £24,000, variable cost per unit of £6 and a selling price of £14. Calculate the break-even point in units, and explain what this figure means for the business.

Hint: Contribution = £14 − £6 = £8. Break-even = £24,000 ÷ £8 = 3,000 units. Explain that the business must sell 3,000 units before it starts making a profit — every unit sold after that adds £8 of pure profit to the bottom line.
Examiner Trap: Students often invert the break-even formula or forget to calculate contribution first (Price − Variable Cost per unit). Another frequent mistake is confusing gross profit and net profit — remember: gross profit is BEFORE overheads, net profit is AFTER. A business can have a strong gross margin but a weak net margin if its overheads are too high.
3.6.4

Analysing Financial Performance

Trends, ratios and stakeholder uses of financial information

Why Analyse Financial Performance?

A single figure — "we made £100,000 profit last year" — tells you almost nothing on its own. Is that good? It depends on how much revenue generated it, how it compares to last year, what competitors achieved, and what industry norms are. Financial analysis turns raw numbers into usable information by comparing them across time, against competitors and against benchmarks.

The Three Lenses of Financial Analysis

1. Over time: Are revenue, profit and margins rising or falling year on year?
2. Against competitors: How does our net margin compare to rivals in the same industry?
3. Against targets: Are we ahead of or behind the business plan's forecast?

Analysing Over Time — Trend Analysis

Looking at a figure alongside the same figure for the previous 2–3 years reveals the direction of travel. A declining net margin over three years, even if the business is still profitable, is a warning sign. A rising margin suggests pricing power, cost discipline or a genuinely improving product.

Falling net margin and falling cash suggest the business is becoming less efficient and more fragile. This is called growth-driven deterioration — a classic warning sign that scale is not translating into strength.

Limitations of Financial Analysis

What Financial Analysis Shows Well

  • Direction of travel (rising or falling profit)
  • Comparison with last year and competitors
  • Ability to meet financial obligations
  • Where costs have risen or revenues fallen
  • How efficiently capital is used

What It Does NOT Show

  • Employee morale and culture
  • Quality of management and leadership
  • Customer satisfaction and brand reputation
  • Risks the business has not yet experienced
  • External factors (competition, regulation, economy)
  • Ethical behaviour of the business
Real-World Example
Wilko — How the Numbers Warned Before Collapse

Wilko, the high-street variety store, entered administration in August 2023, closing all 400 shops and making 12,500 staff redundant. In the three years before collapse, the signals in the accounts were clear: gross margins eroded from around 36% to 31% as discount rivals (B&M, Home Bargains) cut prices; net profit turned into losses of over £35m a year; cash reserves collapsed from £50m to single digits. Yet Wilko continued to pay family dividends of over £70m during this period. Analysis of the numbers would have told any neutral observer that the business was in terminal decline — years before it actually failed.

MCQ — 3.6.4: A business has seen its net profit margin fall from 15% to 8% over three years, while revenue has doubled. This pattern is BEST described as:
  • A) Healthy growth
  • B) Growth-driven deterioration
  • C) A cash flow problem
  • D) A break-even success
Answer: B — The business is growing in size but shrinking in quality. Rising revenue with falling margin typically indicates discounting, rising costs, or growth beyond operational capacity. It is a classic warning pattern.
Past Paper Question — 9 marks

Q: Recommend whether the owners of a business showing rising revenue but falling net profit margin should be worried about its financial health. Give reasons for your recommendation.

Hint: Para 1 — FOR worry: rising revenue with falling margin indicates the business is "buying growth" at an unsustainable cost (discounting, rising variable costs, expanding overheads faster than sales). Para 2 — evaluation: depends on whether the fall is deliberate (investing in expansion) or accidental (losing control). Para 3 — conclusion: NEW info such as whether the business has cash reserves to absorb two more years of margin pressure, or whether competitors are showing similar patterns (industry-wide vs specific).
Examiner Trap: In evaluation questions, students often focus only on one metric (usually profit) and ignore cash, margins and trends. A Grade 9 answer weaves several metrics together. Also remember: revenue growth is NOT the same as business health. A growing but loss-making business can fail just as quickly as a shrinking one — sometimes faster.

9-Mark Exemplar: Should Verve Cafe take a bank loan or use retained profit to fund expansion?

Case Study — Verve Cafe Ltd (practice item)

Verve Cafe Ltd runs a successful single coffee shop in Bristol. Annual revenue is £480,000 with a net profit margin of 9%, giving £43,200 net profit. Retained profit has built up to £60,000 over three years. The owner wants to open a second shop at a cost of £75,000. Option 1 is a 5-year bank loan at 8% interest (monthly repayment £1,520). Option 2 is to use £60,000 retained profit plus a £15,000 overdraft.

Model 9-Mark Answer

Q: Recommend whether Verve Cafe Ltd should fund the new shop through a bank loan or through retained profit plus an overdraft. Give reasons for your recommendation. [9 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Argument FOR retained profit + overdraft (4 chains + data):
An advantage of using retained profit plus an overdraft is that Verve Cafe Ltd would avoid the long-term cost of interest on a loan. The bank loan requires a monthly repayment of £1,520 over 5 years — a total of £91,200 for a £75,000 borrowing. This is because the 8% interest rate adds roughly £16,200 of additional cost that produces no new value for the business. This means that using the £60,000 of retained profit saves the full interest cost, leaving Verve Cafe with a smaller overdraft requirement of just £15,000 to bridge the remaining gap. As a result, the new shop starts generating profit against a much lower cost base — making it easier to reach break-even quickly and reinvest into a possible third location. Therefore, the combined retained profit plus overdraft option protects long-term profitability.

Paragraph 2 — Evaluation (purely evaluative):
However, a more balanced view would recognise that using up the entire £60,000 retained profit leaves Verve Cafe with almost no cash reserve for emergencies. This suggests that a single unexpected shock — a slow opening quarter at the new shop, equipment breakdown at the original shop, or a rent rise — could push the business into a cash crisis. A further limitation is that overdrafts have very high interest rates and can be withdrawn by the bank at any time, so the £15,000 gap is not a stable funding source. It also depends on whether a 5-year loan at 8% is actually a punishing rate — fixed monthly repayments give the business predictability while the alternative leaves it exposed to rising overdraft rates.

Paragraph 3 — Conclusion (NEW information + short/long term):
Overall, I believe Verve Cafe Ltd should fund the expansion through the bank loan — but keep most of the retained profit as a cash reserve. Although the £91,200 total repayment looks expensive compared to using retained profit, this is outweighed by the importance of preserving a financial cushion during the uncertain first year of a new shop. However, this depends on whether Verve Cafe can secure loan terms with an early-repayment option — allowing the business to pay down the loan using excess retained profit once the new shop stabilises. The most important factor is that approximately 60% of new cafes fail within their first three years — so the risk of a cash shortage during launch is significantly higher than the risk of being unable to service a loan with predictable repayments. In the short term the monthly repayment of £1,520 will reduce cash flow, but in the long term the preserved retained profit gives the business resilience, and the business will own both shops outright. Therefore, Verve Cafe should take the bank loan and protect its cash reserve.

12-Mark Exemplar: Cut costs or raise prices to fix a falling net profit margin?

Case Study — Fresco Deli Ltd (practice item)
Fresco Deli Ltd

Fresco Deli Ltd is a 12-shop Mediterranean food chain. Over three years, revenue has grown from £3.8m to £4.5m, but net profit margin has fallen from 11% to 6%. The MD is choosing between two plans to restore the margin.
Option 1 — Cut costs: Reduce staffing in each shop by one role, switch to a cheaper fresh-produce supplier, and cut marketing spend. Forecast saving £280,000 per year.
Option 2 — Raise prices: Increase menu prices by an average of 8%. Forecast revenue impact is uncertain: customer research suggests a 6% fall in customer volume but an overall 1.5% rise in revenue. Additional cost: £40,000 for new menus and signage.

Model 12-Mark Answer

Q: Evaluate which of these two options will have the biggest impact on Fresco Deli Ltd's net profit margin and long-term financial health. [12 marks]

Paragraph 1 — Option 1: Cut costs (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of cutting costs is that Fresco Deli Ltd would see a direct and immediate improvement in its net profit margin. The forecast saving is £280,000 per year. This is because every pound saved from wages, raw materials or marketing falls straight through to net profit — there is no uncertainty about customer reaction. This means that £280,000 added to current net profit of roughly £270,000 (6% of £4.5m) would nearly double net profit and take the margin back above 10% — close to the historic level. As a result, shareholders see a fast recovery in profitability and the business regains financial flexibility. Therefore, cost-cutting is the quickest route to margin recovery. However, this depends on whether the cuts themselves damage the customer experience — a cheaper supplier and fewer staff may hurt the "fresh Mediterranean" brand that customers are paying a premium for.

Evaluation — Option 1:
A limitation is that cost cuts are a one-time lift — once the £280,000 is saved, there is nothing more to cut without destroying the business. It also depends on whether the cheaper supplier can actually deliver the quality Fresco Deli's premium positioning requires — if not, customer loyalty collapses and the savings are dwarfed by lost revenue.

Paragraph 2 — Option 2: Raise prices (4 chains + data + depends on)
An advantage of raising prices is that Fresco Deli Ltd protects the brand without weakening the product. An 8% price rise produces a forecast 1.5% rise in revenue after a 6% volume fall. This is because the remaining customers pay more per visit, and the gross margin improves even more sharply than revenue — fixed costs like rent and management salaries are unchanged, so a larger share of each sale becomes profit. This means that net profit could rise significantly even with fewer customers, while the brand, product and staff experience are all preserved intact. As a result, the business strengthens rather than weakens — exactly the opposite of what happens with cost cutting. Therefore, raising prices protects long-term competitiveness. However, this depends on whether the 6% volume loss is accurate — if actual losses turn out to be 15%, the strategy backfires badly.

Evaluation — Option 2:
A limitation is that raising prices during a cost-of-living squeeze risks losing price-sensitive customers permanently — once they switch to a rival, they rarely return. It also depends on whether rivals (like Leon or Pret) raise prices at the same time — if they hold steady, Fresco loses competitive positioning.

Conclusion — 5 Steps:
To conclude, I think that raising prices will have the biggest impact on Fresco Deli Ltd's margin and long-term financial health. Although cutting costs produces a faster and more predictable £280,000 gain, this is outweighed by the fact that cost cuts eventually damage the brand that justifies Fresco's premium pricing — customers will not pay a premium for a deli that no longer feels premium. However, this depends on whether Fresco Deli can phase the price rise city-by-city to test customer response before rolling it out nationally, minimising the risk of a 15% volume loss. The most important factor is that most food inflation in 2024–25 has been absorbed by Fresco's rivals rather than customers, meaning the market expects a price correction soon — Fresco should move before competitors do, not after. In the short term a price rise risks volume losses and customer complaints, but in the long term it strengthens the brand, protects the product quality, and rebuilds the margin sustainably rather than one-off. Therefore, raising prices is the stronger strategy because it restores the margin without damaging the business that earned it.

Exam Technique Masterclass

How to answer every question type for maximum marks
1–2Marks — State / Identify / List
One word, phrase, or single sentence. No explanation needed.
  • Read ALL four options before selecting (MCQs).
  • Use elimination for MCQs.
  • Never write more than one sentence for 1 mark.
2Marks — Explain / Calculate
Point + development OR show your working for a calculation. ONE chain of analysis.
  • Always write out formula before plugging numbers.
  • Include the unit (£, %, units).
4Marks — Explain / Calculate in context
Calculation + interpretation OR point → explain → impact with THREE chains.
  • For calculations: show working, state answer, explain what it means.
  • Link impact back to the business in the question.
6Marks — Analyse
Point → Evidence → Explain with THREE chains → Link to outcome
  • Use case study evidence in blue.
  • End with a clear outcome for the business.
From 2026, "analyse one reason" now requires TWO developed reasons/points.
9Marks — Recommend
THREE paragraphs: (1) Argument with 4 chains + data manipulation, (2) Purely evaluative — "more balanced view / further limitation / depends on", (3) Conclusion: "Overall, I believe" + NEW information + short/long term + "Therefore".
  • Para 1: Point → Evidence from case study → Data manipulation → Consequence → Therefore. Minimum 4 chain connectors.
  • Para 2: Purely evaluative — weigh risks and limitations.
  • Para 3: Clear recommendation with NEW information not in paragraphs 1 or 2. Must include short-term vs long-term reasoning. No fence-sitting.
  • Conclusion that only repeats paragraph 1 = Level 2 maximum.
12Marks — Evaluate
Para 1: Option 1 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 2: Option 2 — 4 chains + data manipulation + "depends on" + short evaluation. Para 3: Conclusion with 5 steps.
  • BOTH options: 4 chains each. Manipulate data (calculate savings, percentages, compare figures).
  • Each option followed by a short concise evaluation (2–3 sentences).
  • Conclusion 5 steps: (1) "To conclude…" (2) "Although… this is outweighed by…" (3) "However, this depends on…" (4) "The most important factor" + NEW information (5) "Therefore…"
  • NEW information MUST appear in the conclusion — a factor, calculation or stakeholder point not raised in paragraphs 1 or 2.
  • Integrate functional areas — link finance → operations → HR → marketing. Isolated analysis = Level 3 maximum.

Key Terms Glossary — Unit 6

Every definition you need to know for the exam
Finance
The money a business needs to start, operate and grow.
Internal Finance
Money raised from within the business itself (e.g. retained profit, sale of assets).
External Finance
Money raised from outside the business (e.g. bank loan, share capital, grants).
Owner's Capital
Money the owner personally invests into the business.
Retained Profit
Profit kept within the business after tax and dividends for reinvestment.
Overdraft
A short-term bank facility allowing a business to spend more than its account balance.
Bank Loan
A fixed sum borrowed from a bank, repaid with interest over an agreed period.
Mortgage
A long-term loan secured against property.
Share Capital
Money raised by selling shares in the business to investors.
Venture Capital
Investment from professional investors, usually in high-growth start-ups, in exchange for equity.
Crowdfunding
Raising small amounts of money from a large number of people online.
Trade Credit
An arrangement where suppliers allow a business to pay for goods after delivery.
Leasing
Paying a regular fee to use an asset (vehicle, machinery) without owning it.
Grant
Money from government or other bodies that does not need to be repaid.
Cash Flow
The movement of money into and out of a business over a period of time.
Cash Inflow
Money coming into the business (sales, loans, grants, asset sales).
Cash Outflow
Money leaving the business (suppliers, wages, rent, tax).
Net Cash Flow
Total inflows minus total outflows for a period.
Cash Flow Forecast
A prediction of future inflows and outflows for each month ahead.
Opening Balance
The cash in the business at the start of a period.
Closing Balance
The cash in the business at the end of a period (opening + net cash flow).
Revenue
Total money received from sales before costs are deducted (price × quantity).
Fixed Costs
Costs that do not change with output (rent, salaries, insurance).
Variable Costs
Costs that rise and fall with output (raw materials, packaging).
Total Costs
Fixed costs + variable costs for a period.
Gross Profit
Revenue minus cost of sales.
Net Profit
Gross profit minus all other expenses (overheads, interest, tax).
Gross Profit Margin
Gross profit as a percentage of revenue. Shows pricing power.
Net Profit Margin
Net profit as a percentage of revenue. True measure of profitability.
Break-Even Point
The level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs (neither profit nor loss).
Contribution
Selling price minus variable cost per unit. The amount each unit contributes to fixed costs.
Margin of Safety
The difference between actual sales and break-even sales — how far sales can fall before a loss.
Solvency
The ability of a business to meet its long-term financial obligations.
Liquidity
The ability of a business to meet short-term obligations — having enough cash to pay today's bills.

How AI Is Reshaping Finance Careers

Unit 6 teaches you how to interpret financial information — but AI has already automated much of the routine data entry, reconciliation and basic analysis that junior finance roles used to involve. The finance professionals who thrive in the next decade will be those who direct AI rather than compete with it. (Gatsby Benchmark 2)

Roles AI Is Creating
  • Financial Data Scientist
  • Quantitative Analyst (Quant)
  • Algorithmic Audit Specialist
  • FinTech Product Manager
  • ESG & Sustainability Analyst
  • AI Finance Controller
Roles AI Is Disrupting
  • Routine bookkeeping
  • Invoice processing
  • Basic accounts payable / receivable
  • Manual reconciliations
  • Standard tax-return preparation
  • Entry-level audit data testing
What AI Cannot Replace
  • Ethical judgement in audit
  • Investor trust-building
  • Strategic financial advice
  • M&A negotiation
  • Crisis management (e.g. insolvency)
  • Interpreting motives behind numbers
The Nishkam Perspective on AI & Finance

AI can calculate faster than any human — but it cannot be held personally responsible for a financial decision. Every major financial scandal — Enron, Carillion, FTX — ultimately hinged on whether a real human chose to tell the truth. The virtues Nishkam builds in you — honesty, integrity, moral courage — are what regulators, investors and employees will increasingly pay a premium for. Unit 6 is not just exam content — it is preparation for the single most important quality in finance: being trusted with other people's money.

Nishkam Virtues — Alive in Finance Careers

Honesty in Reporting
An auditor who refuses to sign off on accounts containing irregularities — even when it costs their firm a major client. Truth in numbers is the foundation on which every other business relationship is built.
→ Auditing • 3.6.4
Prudence & Diligence
A finance analyst who double-checks every assumption in a model before presenting it to the board — knowing that a single wrong figure can lead to a wrong strategic decision. Financial diligence is invisible when right and catastrophic when wrong.
→ Calculations • 3.6.3
Fairness to Stakeholders
A CFO who insists on fair payment terms to small suppliers even when the business could delay and save cash — recognising the human cost to those suppliers. Ethical finance respects that every pound has a person on the other end of it.
→ Sources • 3.6.1
Courage in a Crisis
A finance business partner who tells the CEO an unwelcome truth about cash flow — because silence now would make the problem far worse in six months. The best finance professionals are not those who agree with leadership, but those leadership learns to trust.
